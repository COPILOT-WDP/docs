## Getting Started

<Accordion title="How much money do I need to start?">
  You can start a Pokémon card business with as little as **$100–$300**, though $500–$1,000 gives you more flexibility and faster growth. The key is starting small, learning the fundamentals, and reinvesting profits rather than overleveraging your initial capital.

  Here's how a minimal starting budget breaks down:
  - **$50–$150** — Initial inventory (bulk lots, a binder from a local seller, or a single booster box)
  - **$0–$30** — Supplies (penny sleeves, top loaders, bubble mailers, small boxes)
  - **$0** — Platform setup (eBay and TCGPlayer accounts are free to create)
  - **$0** — Shipping tools (PirateShip is free)

  The biggest mistake new sellers make is buying too much inventory before they understand what sells. Start narrow, learn your market, and scale with confidence.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Do I need a business license to sell Pokémon cards?">
  In most cases, **no business license is required to start selling casually** — but as your revenue grows, the legal and tax picture changes.

  Here's the practical breakdown:
  - **Casual selling** (flipping personal cards, occasional sales): Generally no license required in most jurisdictions
  - **Consistent income** (regular sales, buying to resell): You may be operating as a sole proprietor, which often doesn't require a formal license but does require reporting income on your taxes
  - **Registered business** (LLC, DBA, or state sales tax permit): Recommended once you're generating meaningful revenue, want to open a business bank account, or need a resale certificate to buy from distributors

  Laws vary by country, state, and city. Consult a local accountant or business advisor to understand your specific obligations — this is not legal or tax advice.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Should I focus on sealed product or singles?">
  Both paths are viable, and most successful sellers eventually do both — but they require different skills and capital.

  **Sealed product** is better if you:
  - Want lower listing complexity (fewer SKUs to manage)
  - Are comfortable holding inventory for weeks or months to time the market
  - Enjoy live selling (sealed product shines on Whatnot)

  **Singles** are better if you:
  - Want faster inventory turnover and more frequent cash flow
  - Are willing to learn card-by-card pricing
  - Are starting with limited capital (you can start with just a binder of cards)

  **Recommendation for beginners:** Start with singles from collections you already own or can buy locally at a discount. Singles teach you pricing, grading, and listing mechanics — the core skills you need regardless of which direction you scale.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How do I know if a card is fake?">
  Counterfeit Pokémon cards are a real concern, especially for high-value modern cards. Develop the habit of checking every card you acquire through these methods:

  1. **The light test** — Hold the card up to a bright light. Authentic Pokémon cards are printed with a black layer sandwiched between two white layers. Fakes often let light through more uniformly.
  2. **Font and text** — Fake cards often have slightly off fonts, incorrect kerning, or blurry text. Compare the card to a known genuine copy.
  3. **Card stock feel** — Real cards have a distinct, slightly rigid feel. Fakes often feel too flimsy, too stiff, or too glossy.
  4. **Holographic pattern** — Fake holos often have a flat, printed-looking holo pattern rather than the depth and texture of genuine Pokémon foil.
  5. **Proxy vs. counterfeit** — Proxies are clearly labeled fakes made for casual play; counterfeits are designed to deceive. Never buy or sell counterfeits.

  When in doubt, compare the card side-by-side with a verified genuine copy, or use community resources like the Pokémon TCG subreddit's authentication threads.
</Accordion>

## Pricing & Selling

<Accordion title="Where do I check card prices?">
  Use multiple sources and always prioritize **sold data** over listed prices:

  - **TCGPlayer Market Price** — the best benchmark for raw singles; reflects actual recent sales
  - **eBay Sold Listings** — essential for graded cards, sealed product, vintage, and anything where TCGPlayer data is thin
  - **130point.com** — aggregates eBay sold data in a cleaner interface for faster research
  - **PriceCharting.com** — useful for historical trends and sealed product valuation

  Never price based solely on what other sellers are *asking* — only base your price on what buyers have actually *paid*.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How do I price cards I've never seen before?">
  When you encounter a card you don't recognize, follow this process:

  1. **Identify the card fully** — set name, collector number (bottom right), rarity symbol, and any special designations (Alt Art, Full Art, Secret Rare)
  2. **Search TCGPlayer** by card name + set to find its Market Price
  3. **Cross-check on eBay Sold Listings** to validate the TCGPlayer data with real transaction history
  4. **Assess your card's condition** honestly against NM/LP/MP grading standards — condition significantly affects value
  5. **Price competitively** — start at or slightly below Market Price to move the card, especially if you're new and have low feedback

  The more cards you price, the faster this process becomes. Within a few months, you'll recognize most cards by sight.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Is it better to auction or use Buy It Now?">
  For most cards and most sellers, **Buy It Now (BIN) with Best Offer enabled** is the superior format. Here's why:

  - BIN gives you price certainty — you never sell below your floor
  - Adding Best Offer invites engagement without obligating you to accept lowball bids
  - BIN listings accumulate watchers over time and can close at any moment, whereas auctions have a fixed window
  - BIN is more predictable for business planning and cash flow

  **Auctions work well when:**
  - You have a rare, high-demand card where competitive bidding could push the price above market value
  - You're testing a card's true market value for the first time
  - You want to guarantee a sale by a specific date

  As a rule of thumb: BIN everything standard; auction only the exceptional.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How long should I wait before lowering a price?">
  A general framework: give a card **2–4 weeks** at its initial price before considering a reduction. Here's how to think about it:

  - **High-demand cards** (staples, recent hits): If it hasn't sold in 7–10 days, you may be slightly above market — drop 5–10%
  - **Moderate cards**: 2–4 weeks is reasonable before reassessing
  - **Low-demand cards**: These can sit for months; price them right initially and be patient, or bundle them into a lot

  Always re-check Market Price before adjusting — the market may have moved since you listed. Use eBay's "Sell Similar" feature to relist at the new price rather than simply editing, as fresh listings often get a temporary visibility boost.
</Accordion>

## Shipping & Logistics

<Accordion title="What's the best way to ship a single card?">
  Your shipping method should match the card's value:

  **Low-value cards (under ~$5):**
  - **PWE (Plain White Envelope)** — sleeve the card, place between two rigid pieces of cardboard cut from a cereal box or similar, tape securely, ship first class. This keeps costs at $0.63–$0.73 (a single stamp) but offers no tracking and no real protection against loss.

  **Mid-value cards ($5–$50):**
  - **Bubble mailer with top loader** — sleeve the card, insert into a top loader, place in a team bag, and ship in a padded bubble mailer via USPS First Class Package (with tracking). This is the industry standard for most singles.

  **High-value cards ($50+):**
  - **Rigid mailer or small box** with top loader + team bag, shipping via USPS Priority Mail or with added insurance. Always purchase tracking and consider adding signature confirmation for cards over $250.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Do I need to insure every shipment?">
  No — insuring every shipment is unnecessary and expensive. Apply this framework:

  - **Under $50:** Tracking alone is sufficient. USPS First Class includes $0 insurance by default, but the cost of filing claims makes it not worth adding for low-value items.
  - **$50–$200:** Consider USPS built-in insurance ($2–$5 typically) for peace of mind, especially for graded cards.
  - **$200+:** Always add insurance and consider signature confirmation. Ship via USPS Priority Mail (which includes $100 in free insurance) and purchase additional coverage for the remaining value.

  Note: eBay's Money Back Guarantee and platform protections cover many buyer claims even without insurance — what insurance protects is *your* loss when a package is genuinely lost or damaged in transit.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How do I handle an INR (item not received) claim?">
  Stay calm — INR claims are a normal part of selling at volume. Here's your action plan:

  **Step 1: Check tracking.** Pull up the tracking number and verify the package's status. If it shows "Delivered," you have strong grounds to dispute the claim.

  **Step 2: Contact the buyer.** Send a polite, professional message asking them to check with neighbors, their mailbox/parcel locker, and their local post office. Many "INR" cases resolve this way.

  **Step 3: Wait the platform's required window.** eBay requires waiting a set number of days after the estimated delivery date before a case can be opened.

  **Step 4: If the package is genuinely lost.** Issue a refund or replacement promptly. A lost package without tracking means the platform will almost always side with the buyer — which is why shipping with tracking on everything above PWE value is non-negotiable.

  **Prevention:** Ship with tracking on all orders above $5, and use PirateShip or eBay labels to ensure tracking is automatically uploaded to the order.
</Accordion>

## Grading

<Accordion title="Is it worth getting cards graded?">
  Grading is worth it in specific circumstances — and a costly mistake in others. Ask yourself three questions:

  1. **Is the card valuable enough?** Grading typically costs $20–$50+ per card (plus shipping and insurance). A card needs realistic graded sale value well above that cost to justify submission.
  2. **Is the card in high enough condition?** Submitting a card that comes back as a PSA 7 instead of a 9 can result in a loss. Only submit cards you believe are PSA 8.5+ or BGS 9+.
  3. **Is there a meaningful grade premium?** Some cards see 5x or 10x value jumps between raw and PSA 10. Others barely move. Research completed eBay sales for graded vs. raw copies before committing.

  **Best candidates for grading:** Base Set holos, vintage WOTC cards, popular Alt Arts, and key chase cards from modern sets — especially if you pulled them from a pack or acquired them in demonstrably excellent condition.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Which grading company should I use?">
  The right choice depends on your goals:

  - **PSA** — Choose PSA for maximum resale value and market recognition. PSA 10s command the highest prices across nearly all Pokémon cards. Wait times vary; check current turnaround times before submitting.
  - **BGS** — Choose BGS if you're submitting cards for high-end collectors who value the subgrade transparency. BGS 9.5 and 10 are extremely prestigious. BGS tends to grade Pokémon slightly harder than PSA.
  - **CGC** — Choose CGC for competitive pricing, faster turnaround, and growing market acceptance. CGC is a strong choice for modern cards where you want speed and value without waiting on PSA backlogs.

  For most new sellers, **PSA** remains the safest choice for maximizing resale value on the cards most worth grading.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How do I prepare cards for submission?">
  Proper preparation improves your chances of a high grade. Follow these steps:

  1. **Handle cards by their edges only** — fingerprints add surface scratches and reduce your grade
  2. **Clean your cards gently** — use a microfiber cloth to remove dust before sleeving; never use liquid cleaners
  3. **Sleeve cards in a perfect-fit sleeve** first, then place inside a semi-rigid card saver (not a hard top loader, which PSA prefers you avoid)
  4. **Bundle card savers** with rubber bands for submission — follow each grading company's specific packaging instructions exactly
  5. **Photograph every card** at high resolution before shipping your submission — document the condition you sent
  6. **Ship your submission** via USPS Priority Mail with tracking and insurance; declare the full value of the cards

  Read the submission guidelines on the grading company's website carefully before your first submission — requirements change and vary by company.
</Accordion>

## Growing the Business

<Accordion title="When should I start selling on multiple platforms?">
  Expand to a second platform once you've **mastered the fundamentals on your first**. Rushing to list everywhere at once is a common mistake that leads to overselling (committing to sales you can't fulfill), inconsistent service, and burnout.

  Use this checklist before adding a second platform:
  - You're consistently shipping within your stated handling time
  - Your feedback score is positive and growing
  - You've developed a repeatable listing workflow
  - You have enough inventory to support two active storefronts

  **Recommended progression:** Start with eBay → add TCGPlayer → add Whatnot (if you want live selling) → build Shopify (once you have a customer base to bring with you).
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How do I get my first reviews?">
  Reviews are the lifeblood of a new seller's credibility. Here's how to accelerate them legitimately:

  1. **Ship fast** — same day or next business day shipping earns positive feedback before buyers even open the package
  2. **Pack carefully** — a well-packaged order signals professionalism and builds buyer confidence
  3. **Include a thank-you note** — a simple handwritten or printed note asking the buyer to leave feedback if they're happy with their order is effective and allowed on most platforms
  4. **Follow up** — on eBay, you can send a polite post-purchase message thanking the buyer and reminding them to leave feedback
  5. **Leave feedback for buyers first** — on eBay, many buyers reciprocate when sellers leave feedback proactively

  Never offer incentives (discounts, free cards) in exchange for positive reviews — this violates platform terms of service.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What do I do with bulk common cards?">
  Bulk commons are inevitable, and there are several ways to turn them into revenue rather than clutter:

  - **Sell as bulk lots on eBay** — "100 random Pokémon cards" lots are consistently popular with parents buying for kids, beginner collectors, and school teachers. Price at $5–$15 per 100 cards depending on set era and quality.
  - **Offer bulk on TCGPlayer** — Even common cards sell on TCGPlayer at $0.05–$0.25 each when listed individually; the volume adds up if you can batch-list efficiently.
  - **Sell to bulk buyers on Facebook Groups** — Pokémon TCG buy/sell/trade groups often have buyers looking for bulk by the pound or per-thousand-card lots.
  - **Use as filler in multi-card orders** — including a few random bonus cards with a purchase costs you nothing and delights buyers, increasing the chance of positive feedback.
  - **Donate to local schools or community centers** — worth considering for the lowest-value commons when the revenue potential doesn't justify the time.
</Accordion>
