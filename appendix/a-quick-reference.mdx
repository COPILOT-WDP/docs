Documented operator practice, US market, 2025–2026. Treat every number as a starting point
and verify current fees on your venue — the *categories* are durable, the digits drift
(Chapter 2.2: learn categories, not fee tables).

## The engine

| Number | Rule | Full table |
|---|---|---|
| **~70%** of market | The buy anchor for collections — adjust by band, for a reason | 2.1 |
| **~95%** of market | The sell price that keeps inventory moving | 2.4 |
| **~8% / ~15%** of gross | Lean vs. typical all-in cost stack — measure yours | 2.2 |
| **7–8% per turn** | Rotated fast, beats holding for appreciation | 1.1, 2.3 |
| Sales × **0.15–0.25** | Honest earnings conversion — never quote sales as profit | 2.4 |

## The bands (from Chapter 2.1)

70% standard · up to 75% learning or volume · 75–80% itemized clean lots · 80%+ only with
proven velocity and a lean stack · dirt-cheap-or-pass for anything that sits · ~90%+
vendor-to-vendor · bulk is cents-per-card, not percentages.

## The bankroll ladder (from Chapters 3.2 / 5.2)

$20–100 bulk (the school) → $200–500 first collections → $2,000–4,000 steady collection
buying → $3,000–5,000 liquid sealed → five-figure buys for established stores with reserves.
All of it cash. None of it rent money.

## Packaging tiers (from Chapter 4.4)

~$0.85 windowed envelope → team bag + bubble mailer around $60–70 orders → signature at $150
→ verification call at $300–500 → boxed tiers for sealed. Pack to the price, not the card.

## The fully-loaded hour (from Chapter 4.2)

A one-day show = 25–30 total hours with prep and travel. Price your hours in every venue
decision — and in every chaos-lot purchase (2.1).

## The four classes at a glance

Singles = granular, labor-priced. Sealed = capital in a box, chunky liquidity. Bulk = a
school with a stipend. Slabs = high ticket, capital in a queue. Full matrix: Chapter 3.1.
Match to your bankroll and calendar: Chapter 3.2.
