Sourcing is the foundation of a profitable Pokémon business. Without a reliable pipeline of quality inventory at the right prices, every other part of your operation — grading, listing, shipping — becomes irrelevant. Before you spend a single dollar, take the time to understand where cards come from, which channels offer the best margins, and how to diversify so a single dry spell doesn't stall your entire business.

## Retail Arbitrage

Retail arbitrage means buying product from traditional retailers at a discount and reselling it at a profit. Target, Walmart, and GameStop are your three primary hunting grounds. Check clearance aisles and end caps regularly — seasonal clearance events (post-holiday, back-to-school) often yield Elite Trainer Boxes and booster packs at 30–50% off retail. Use a barcode-scanning app to quickly compare shelf prices against current eBay sold listings before you buy. Build relationships with employees at your local stores so you get early notice of markdowns. Consistency is key: visit frequently rather than hoping for a single big score.

## Online Marketplaces

The internet is the largest secondhand Pokémon marketplace in the world. On **eBay**, search "Pokémon lot" under sold listings to see what bulk collections actually sell for — then look for active listings priced below that threshold. **Facebook Marketplace** and **OfferUp** surface local sellers who often don't know the value of what they have. **TCGPlayer** offers bulk purchasing directly from other sellers, and its market-price data is one of the most accurate pricing references available. When buying lots online, always review the seller's feedback, ask for additional photos, and factor shipping costs into your margin calculation before committing.

## Card Shows and Conventions

Card shows and local gaming conventions are among the best sourcing environments for serious resellers. Dealers at shows are often willing to negotiate, especially late in the day when they're trying to reduce the weight they carry home. More importantly, shows attract private collectors looking to liquidate entire binders or boxes at once — these raw collection purchases can be your highest-margin deals. Come prepared with a phone loaded with price-check apps, a loupe or jeweler's glass for condition checks, and cash for faster negotiations. Introduce yourself to recurring dealers; a reputation as a fair buyer who pays quickly opens doors to deals before they hit the public floor.

## Local Buy / Facebook

Facebook Buy/Sell/Trade groups dedicated to Pokémon in your region are a goldmine for motivated sellers. Parents liquidating childhood collections, adults who played during the original era, and casual collectors who quit the hobby all end up here. Post a standing "WTB: Pokémon Collections" ad with clear pricing criteria so sellers come to you. Respond quickly — speed matters when multiple buyers are competing for the same lot. Meet in safe, public locations and always inspect cards before paying. Local deals eliminate shipping costs entirely, which can make marginally thin deals worth taking.

## Wholesalers and Distributors

Licensed Pokémon distributors sell sealed product (booster boxes, ETBs, collections) to retailers at wholesale pricing — typically 20–35% below MSRP depending on volume. To access these accounts, you generally need a registered business entity and a resale certificate. Distributors like **Alliance Game Distributors**, **GTS Distribution**, and **ACD Distribution** are common entry points in the U.S. Be aware of minimum order quantities (MOQs), which can range from a few hundred to several thousand dollars per order. Wholesale sourcing works best for sealed product resale and is less suited for singles arbitrage.

## Estate Sales and Thrift Stores

Estate sales and thrift stores like Goodwill and Savers occasionally surface Pokémon collections priced far below market value. These are high-effort, low-frequency channels — but the margins when you find something can be extraordinary. Use estate-sale listing sites like **EstateSales.net** and **Estatesale.com** to preview upcoming sales and filter for listings that mention "cards," "collectibles," or "games." At thrift stores, check the toy and game sections on a regular schedule; restocking is random and frequent visits increase your odds. The downside is unpredictability — you cannot build a business solely on thrift finds, but they make an excellent supplement to more reliable channels.

<Tip>
  The most resilient Pokémon businesses combine at least three sourcing channels. Retail arbitrage provides steady volume; local buying and estate sales deliver high-margin finds; and online marketplaces keep your pipeline full between in-person opportunities.
</Tip>

<Warning>
  Avoid purchasing Pokémon cards from unverified overseas suppliers on platforms like Alibaba or Wish. These channels are a primary source of counterfeit product, including fake holos and proxy rares that are nearly impossible to sell once identified. Always verify the authenticity of any overseas supply before placing a large order.
</Warning>
