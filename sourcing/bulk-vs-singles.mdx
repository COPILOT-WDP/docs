Choosing between bulk lots and individual singles is one of the most important strategic decisions you'll make as a Pokémon reseller. Each approach has fundamentally different capital requirements, skill demands, and profit curves. Bulk buying rewards speed and volume; singles demand precise market knowledge and patience. Most successful sellers don't pick one and ignore the other — but understanding the tradeoffs clearly helps you decide where to put your energy and money first.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Bulk Lots">
    ## What Are Bulk Lots?

    Bulk lots are large collections of cards sold together — typically anywhere from 100 cards to several thousand — without individual pricing. They come from private sellers liquidating childhood collections, estate sales, Facebook Marketplace listings, and eBay. The buyer accepts the lot as-is and then sorts through to find value.

    ### Pros

    - **Lower cost per card.** Bulk lots regularly price at $0.01–$0.10 per card, giving you a low cost basis on the entire inventory.
    - **Faster sourcing.** One purchase fills your inventory quickly instead of hunting down individual cards one by one.
    - **Easier negotiation.** Private sellers often have no idea what they have, creating room for favorable pricing.
    - **Learning opportunity.** Sorting bulk exposes you to hundreds of sets, card names, and price points — an accelerated education in the market.

    ### Cons

    - **Sorting labor.** Every card must be identified, priced, and sleeved before it can be listed. A 1,000-card lot can take 3–6 hours to sort properly.
    - **Mixed quality.** Bulk lots frequently contain heavily played or damaged cards that have little or no resale value, reducing effective yield.
    - **Unpredictable value.** Until you sort a lot, you don't know exactly what you have. Accurate pre-purchase valuation depends on sampling or a full card list from the seller.
    - **Storage requirements.** Bulk inventory takes up significant physical space before it's sorted and listed.

    ### Best For

    Bulk lots are the ideal starting point for **beginners**. The low per-card cost limits downside risk while you develop your eye for card values, condition assessment, and the sorting workflow that underpins the entire business.
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Singles">
    ## What Are Singles?

    Singles are individual cards purchased specifically because you know their market value and have a buyer in mind — or high confidence one exists. You might buy a PSA 10 Charizard, a playset of competitive tournament staples, or a specific vintage holo because you've identified a gap between the price you can acquire it at and the price the market will pay.

    ### Pros

    - **Known value upfront.** You research the card before you buy it, so there are no sorting surprises — you know exactly what you're getting.
    - **Targeted buyers.** High-value singles attract motivated buyers who search specifically for that card, reducing time-to-sale.
    - **Higher per-card margins.** A single $200 card can generate more absolute profit than sorting an entire 1,000-card bulk lot.
    - **Compact inventory.** Singles take up very little storage space relative to their value.

    ### Cons

    - **Higher upfront cost.** Quality singles require significant capital per unit, especially in the graded or vintage market.
    - **Slower turnover.** Not every single sells quickly. Niche cards can sit in inventory for weeks or months before the right buyer appears.
    - **Requires deep market knowledge.** Mispricing a single by even 10–15% can wipe out your margin or result in a loss once fees are applied.
    - **Condition sensitivity.** A single card's grade determines its value dramatically — buying a card that turns out to be NM- instead of NM can cost you significantly.

    ### Best For

    Singles buying rewards **experienced sellers** who have invested time in learning price trends, condition grading, and the specific buyer demand for individual cards across sets and eras.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## The Hybrid Strategy

The most efficient approach combines both methods. When you purchase a bulk lot, you sort it into tiers: pull every card worth $1 or more and list those as individual singles on TCGPlayer or eBay. Cards worth $0.25–$0.99 can be listed in small lots or sold to other dealers. The remaining commons and uncommons get bundled into 100-card or 500-card bulk lots and resold as-is. This way you extract maximum value from every purchase while also building a singles inventory without paying singles prices to acquire it.

## Strategy Comparison

| | Bulk Lots | Singles |
|---|---|---|
| **Startup Cost** | Low ($25–$200 per lot) | High ($50–$500+ per card) |
| **Sorting Required** | Yes — extensive | Minimal |
| **Profit Margin** | 50–200% on pulled singles | 20–60% per card |
| **Best Platforms** | eBay, Facebook, local | TCGPlayer, eBay, COMC |
| **Skill Level** | Beginner-friendly | Intermediate to Advanced |

<Tip>
  Start with bulk lots for your first 90 days. The sorting process is the fastest way to learn which cards are valuable, which sets are popular, and how condition affects price — market knowledge you'll need before you start paying singles prices for individual cards.
</Tip>

<Note>
  Track your inventory differently depending on your strategy. For singles, maintain a card-by-card inventory log with purchase price, acquisition date, and listed price. For bulk lots, track cost per lot, estimated yield value after sorting, and actual realized revenue once sold. Both records are essential for calculating your true margins and informing future buying decisions.
</Note>
