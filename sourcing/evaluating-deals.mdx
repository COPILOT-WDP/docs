Not every cheap lot is a good deal — price alone tells you almost nothing. A $50 collection looks attractive until you realize the cards are worth $60 at retail, platform fees eat $8, and shipping another $7, leaving you with a $5 loss before your time. Profitable sourcing requires you to calculate your actual net margin on every deal before you commit, using real sold data, accurate fee estimates, and a disciplined offer formula. Build that habit early and it will save you from the most common mistake new resellers make: confusing "low price" with "good deal."

## The Deal Evaluation Formula

The core math behind every purchase decision is straightforward:

```
(Expected Sale Price × Quantity) - Platform Fees - Shipping - Cost of Goods = Net Profit
```

Work this formula from the outside in. Start with what the cards will actually sell for (not list price — sold price), subtract every cost that stands between you and the cash, and what remains is your real profit. If the margin is below 20–25%, the deal typically isn't worth the time and risk unless it's extremely high volume.

## Understanding Platform Fees

Fees reduce your margin faster than almost anything else. Build them into every calculation — never treat your gross revenue as profit.

| Platform | Selling Fee | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| eBay | ~13.25% | Includes final value fee; varies slightly by category |
| TCGPlayer | ~12.5% | Plus applicable shipping label costs |
| PayPal (off-platform) | ~3% | Add when accepting direct PayPal payments |
| Facebook Marketplace | 5% (shipped) | Free for local pickup |

Always add estimated shipping costs on top of fees. For small lots, factor $4–6 for a bubble mailer; for larger collections, $10–15 for a box is a reasonable baseline.

## How to Evaluate a Deal Step by Step

<Steps>
  <Step title="Check Sold Comps">
    Pull sold listings for every noteworthy card in the lot on eBay and TCGPlayer. Record the average sold price for each card in the past 30 days — ignore outliers in either direction.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Calculate Total Expected Value">
    Multiply each card's average sold price by its quantity and sum the totals. This is your gross expected revenue if every card sells at market price.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Subtract Fees and Shipping">
    Apply your platform fee percentage to the gross revenue total. Add outbound shipping costs for each lot or individual card you expect to ship. This gives you your net revenue.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Make Your Offer at 40–60% of Net Value">
    Offer between 40% and 60% of your net revenue figure as your maximum buy price. This built-in buffer covers unsold cards, condition downgrades, and your time. For riskier lots with uncertain condition, stay closer to 40%.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Red Flags in a Lot

Walk away — or significantly reduce your offer — when you spot any of the following:

<Accordion title="Water Damage or Humidity Warping">
  Wavy cards, soft corners, or a musty smell are signs of moisture exposure. Moisture damage is permanent and dramatically reduces resale value. Even a single warped card in an otherwise clean lot should prompt closer inspection of everything.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Suspected Counterfeits">
  Fake holos, proxies, and counterfeit rares are common in bulk lots. Check card texture (real cards have a distinct dot-matrix pattern on the back), font consistency on the card name, and whether the card bends and snaps back correctly. When in doubt, don't buy.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Inconsistent Card Condition">
  If the seller shows only a few cards from a large lot, ask for photos of a random sample. Sellers who only photograph the best cards in a collection often bury heavily played or damaged cards in the middle of the stack.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Overly Vague Descriptions">
  Listings that say "huge lot, lots of rares!" without any card list or photos are high-risk. A legitimate seller with good cards will be willing to provide specifics. Vague descriptions often signal a lack of valuable cards or undisclosed condition issues.
</Accordion>

## Quick Visual Inspection Checklist

When you receive a lot or are inspecting in person, run through this checklist before finalizing the deal:

- [ ] Check all four corners for whitening or nicks
- [ ] Hold cards under direct light to check for scratches on holos
- [ ] Inspect card backs for creases, bends, or ink rubs
- [ ] Smell the lot for mustiness (moisture damage indicator)
- [ ] Compare card thickness to a known authentic card
- [ ] Verify set symbols and card numbering match official print runs

<Note>
  For sealed product (booster boxes, ETBs), apply the **3x rule of thumb**: only buy sealed product if you can acquire it for one-third or less of the current market price. This accounts for the risk of opening (no guaranteed hits), platform fees, and slower sell-through compared to singles.
</Note>

<Tip>
  Build a simple spreadsheet template with columns for Card Name, Quantity, Avg Sold Price, Total Value, Platform Fee %, Shipping Estimate, and Max Offer. Running every deal through the same template takes two minutes and keeps your decision-making consistent — especially when a seller is pressuring you for a quick answer.
</Tip>
