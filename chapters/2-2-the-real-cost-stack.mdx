An operator put his monthly report on screen. Storefront channel: $17,914 in sales, $987 in
fees. Marketplace channel: $7,400 in sales — $1,123 in fees. More fees on seven thousand of
sales than on eighteen. Same cards, same month, same hands packing the envelopes.

That gap isn't luck. It's the cost stack — the second most important number in your business,
right after the price you pay.

## The principle

**The stack gets paid first.** Gross spread — market value minus your buy price — is not
profit. Before a dollar reaches you, every sale pays a queue of bills: the platform, the tax
line, the postage meter, the supply shelf, the buyer who returns, the buyer who lies, and your
own hours. One operator's beginner warning: buy an elite trainer box for $100, sell it for
$110, and "you didn't make $110, you made $10, maybe even less" — after fees and shipping,
maybe nothing.

Chapter 2.1 planned around the stack as a bundle: lean setups near 8% of gross, typical ones
near 15%. This chapter unbundles it, because the stack decides everything upstream. When one
operator pays 80 or 85% of market, he has to charge full market — "you gotta think there's
taxes, there's fees," chargeback risk, the rest. A heavy stack on a heavy buy doesn't just
shrink profit; it takes away your right to price for speed. Chapter 2.1 told you what that
earns: a hobby with extra steps.

One instruction first: **learn categories, not fee tables.** Rates drift: operators documented
one marketplace raising commission from 10.25% to 10.75% and its fee cap from $50 to $75, and
a social platform going 2% → 6% → "13% plus some" within months. The categories are
permanent; the percentages have expiration dates.

## The seven categories

| # | Category | What it actually is | Documented reality |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Platform fees** | Commission plus payment processing on every sale | 13–15% on big marketplaces; ~5% on a seller storefront; 2.5–3% on your own site; up to 50% on sub-$3 cards in a fulfillment program |
| 2 | **Sales taxes** | Collected and remitted — by the platform, or by you | One own-store operator pays 6.1% of gross to his state; a retail-flip example budgets 7%; sales tax alone killed one documented arbitrage |
| 3 | **Shipping** | The gap between what the buyer pays and what the label costs | $0.86 plain envelope; $5.50–6 tracked mailer; $6–9 to box an ETB; "Even charging you $10, $12, I lose almost every single time on shipping" |
| 4 | **Supplies** | Boxes, mailers, wrap, tape — per order, forever | 66¢ small boxes, $4 big ones; first packaging order $700–800; breaking even on postage still loses money on the box |
| 5 | **Damage & returns** | Refunds you absorb; inventory that comes back worse | One month showed $2,800 in refunds; platforms side with the buyer; damaged sealed sells 5–10% under market |
| 6 | **Chargebacks & fraud** | The sale that becomes a loss of the whole item | "Chargebacks are inevitable at some point"; a scam on an item bought at $80 loses $80, against a best case of +$20 |
| 7 | **Your time** | Hours, priced at a wage | 25–30 hours all-in for a one-day show; 12–14 hours to net ~$3,000; ~800 hours for a $100,000 collection |

Categories 1–6 are percentages of gross; category 7 is priced in hours, which is why the
8–15% planning range never includes it — price your hours separately (Chapter 2.1). Both
prices have to clear.

And do the category-6 arithmetic beginners skip: fraud costs cost basis, not margin. Buy a
$100 item at 80% and your best case is +$20; one chargeback is −$80. That asymmetry is why one
operator past $50,000 a month runs a fraud-screening app and searches buyers' addresses before
shipping — and why "chargeback risk" sits inside the 80–85% rule above. Even a cancelled order
can eat a 3% processing fee, turning an 80% buy into 83%.

## The stack varies by venue — and by volume

| Venue archetype | Fee line (documented) | What else moves |
|---|---|---|
| Marketplace (TCGplayer/eBay-class) | 13–15% | Demand built in; sales tax mostly handled; fees fall with seller level — "the real established ones aren't paying 15%" |
| Seller storefront / pro tier | ~5% total | Same rails, but you bring the buyer |
| Your own website | 2.5–3% processing + ~$30/month | You own the customer — plus sales-tax remittance and fraud screening |
| Live & social selling | One documented run: 2% → 6% → 13% in months | High velocity; schedules churn fastest here |
| Shows and local | 2–2.5% card processing, 0% on cash; tables $50–$165, $300–$1,000 at majors | The real cost is hours — 25–30 all-in; ~$70/hour documented, before inventory cost |

Volume bends every line: fee tiers drop as your seller level rises, a $600 pallet of boxes
cuts per-order supply cost, and one operator's label software runs about 60% off retail
postage. Item price bends hardest of all: in one fulfillment program, cards under $3 carry 50%
fees — a 40¢ sale keeps 20¢. "Is my stack 8 or 15?" has no single answer — compute it for
the venue, volume, and price band you'll start in.

> **Operators disagree.** One school charges flat shipping and keeps life simple: $10 an
> order, "losing a few bucks shipping to Maine, that's okay" — the ledger breaks even overall
> and simplicity converts. The other school got burned into surgery: after losing about $5 per
> coast-to-coast order, one operator set $12 far-coast minimums and a $15 Hawaii floor —
> and still eats $3–4 there sometimes. Resolve it with one question: **does your
> monthly shipping ledger — collected versus spent — net near zero?** Charge flat
> until the ledger objects; go surgical where the map keeps biting.

## Tuition: shipping bleeds first

Shipping is where documented beginners paid the most tuition. "I lost stupid money my first
couple days of the store because I put a flat rate of like $10" — then a California customer
bought 20–30 ETBs and the label ran $32 against $10 collected. A free-shipping promotion at
another operator's first event lost about $300. Retail boxes at $3 apiece can make a fine
order unprofitable on packaging alone.

Two lessons. The category is learnable: the same operators later tuned rates by region, bought
box pallets, switched label software. And fix categories, not pennies: one operator warns
beginners against agonizing over 2¢ versus 5¢ top loaders while the real leaks are a fee tier
and a rate table. Pennies only become a category at bulk volume, where fee floors on forty-cent
cards are the whole game.

## Worksheet: your stack before your first buy

Fill in the last column with real numbers — your venue's current fee schedule and an actual
label quote, not vibes — before your first purchase. Write the date on it. The reference
columns are planning bundles from the numbers above.

| Line | Lean reference | Typical reference | Yours |
|---|---|---|---|
| Platform fees + processing | 5% | 13% | ___ |
| Shipping gap (labels − what buyers pay, ÷ gross) | ~1.5% | ~0.5% | ___ |
| Supplies (÷ gross) | ~0.5% | ~0.5% | ___ |
| Damage / returns / chargeback allowance | ~1% | ~1% | ___ |
| Sales tax you remit (usually 0 on marketplaces) | 0% | 0% | ___ |
| **Cost stack** | **~8%** | **~15%** | ___ |

(Operator experience, not advice: one own-store seller pays 6.1% of gross to his state;
another warns that $100,000 of sales into one state can trigger registration there. If you're
the tax collector, add your state's line — and ask a professional.)

Now use it. You'll sell at 95% of market (Chapter 2.4), so:

**Max buy % = 95 − (95 × stack) − the points you demand for your work.**

Demand a modest 10 points. Typical stack: 95 − 14 − 10 ≈ **70%**. Lean stack: 95 − 8 − 10 ≈
**77%**.

The 70% anchor from Chapter 2.1 isn't folklore — it's what the arithmetic returns when a
typical stack meets a seller who insists on being paid. The operator paying 77–80% is running
the same formula with a 5% fee line. Different stack, different band, same math.

## In practice

Before your first buy — and again every quarter:

- [ ] Have I written down my venue's current fee schedule, dated today?
- [ ] Do I know my shipping gap — collected versus spent — or at least a real label quote
  for my typical order?
- [ ] Does my math carry an allowance for damage, returns, and chargebacks — or does it assume
  nobody lies and nothing breaks?
- [ ] On my own store: do I know who remits sales tax, me or the platform — confirmed with a
  professional?
- [ ] Does my computed max buy percentage match the band I'd have offered in Chapter 2.1?
- [ ] Have I priced my hours at a wage — any wage — and does the deal still clear?

**The handle to remember:** the stack gets paid first. You make money when you buy — the stack
decides how much of it you keep.
