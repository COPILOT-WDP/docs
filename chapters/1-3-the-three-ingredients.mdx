One operator has a test for would-be sellers. Your New Year's resolution was to start a
Pokemon card business. It's June. Half a year in, all you've built is a bad storefront
website. His verdict, lightly cleaned up: this business is not for you — and that's okay.

Notice what the website was doing. It wasn't selling cards. It was standing in for selling
cards.

## The principle

**A card business needs exactly three things: time, knowledge, and inventory.** An operator
with a six-figure side hustle says it in those words — "you need time, you need
knowledge, and you need inventory" — and the corollary that matters: **miss one of the
three and it costs you in the other two.**

The loop is buy cards, know what they're worth, list them, ship them, repeat. Inventory feeds
the loop, knowledge prices it, time runs it. Nothing else on the "real business" checklist —
entity paperwork, hardware, subscriptions, branding — produces an order. Those are
amplifiers. You buy amplifiers after there's a signal.

## The don't-need list

Every row below is a documented reason a real person delayed listing a first card.

| "First I need..." | What operators actually did | When it earns its place |
|---|---|---|
| **An LLC** | On record: "no, you don't need one." A sole proprietor with a personal checking account can sell today. | Operators split on timing — several thousand in sales vs. as soon as possible (Chapter 5.1). |
| **A card scanner** ($650–700) | One operator passed $300,000 in sales before owning one. | When listing is a proven bottleneck — a couple thousand dollars in sales, a couple hundred orders. |
| **Inventory software** | "You probably don't even need a software until you have tons and tons of sales." Phone-camera scanning works. | At volume. Tools amplify systems; they don't create them (Chapter 4.3). |
| **A polished website, business cards, logo, graphics** | Documented stalling excuses — verbatim: "I probably shouldn't launch that website because it doesn't look perfect yet." | Polish never gates the first listing. Your own site is a channel choice for later (Chapter 4.2). |
| **$30,000** | "You likely don't just have $30,000" — and, in the same breath: it isn't required. | It never does. Capital is a lever, not a ticket. |

That pattern has a documented name: perfectionism. Perfection doesn't
motivate action — it postpones it. Every added prerequisite "just continues moving the
starting line down the road," and you can't get better from not starting. The operation
needn't look official either: one operator runs a side hustle nearing $500,000 in sales
from a basement floor, on a kitchen food scale.

When money is the stated blocker, one operator's rule has no padding: if roughly $30 a
month for a Shopify/Wix-class storefront feels cost-prohibitive, don't start a business — do
you not believe you'll make more than $30 a month? You don't need the storefront (see the
table); the rule is a belief test, and no purchase fixes belief.

## Pricing the three ingredients honestly

**Time.** The documented starting dose is a couple of hours a day — evenings, after the
full-time job, after the kids are in bed. Nearly everyone starts part-time (Chapter 1.2). The
honest question isn't "do I have time?" It's "where is this time going to come from?"
Something on your calendar loses.

**Knowledge.** The game is thirty years old with over 200,000 unique English printings. Your
job is to know which cards are good buys and which will sell — nobody starts knowing. You buy
this ingredient with tuition: one operator lost about $5 a box on a Japanese release because
he didn't yet know those prices sag after launch. Breaking even while you learn is fine —
you're "working for free tuition" in buying, negotiating, listing, condition, what sells and
what sits (Chapter 1.2's tuition period).

**Inventory.** The ingredient people assume requires a fortune. It doesn't — you are probably
already sitting on it.

The three trade against each other. Short on capital? Spend time: $20 to $100 of bulk buys
thousands of cards to sort, list, and learn condition on (Chapter 3.1 calls bulk the school
of the business). Short on knowledge? Pay tuition in small, survivable mistakes. Short on
time? The trade runs backward — one stretched-thin operator says bulk eats hours better spent
on higher-profit work. You can trade between ingredients. You cannot skip one.

## Your collection is your seed round

Where does first inventory come from? The documented answer: "the first place is your own
collection." Most would-be sellers already own a binder worth a few hundred to a few thousand
dollars — sometimes with $300–$500 single cards. Selling the cards you no longer love
converts a shelf into starting capital; one operator seeded his store's early sales by
selling a complete master set.

What the seed opens:

| Your seed | What it opens |
|---|---|
| $20–$100 | Bulk to sort and list — thousands of cards of practice for the price of a dinner out. |
| $200–$500 | The working range: at the low end you're sifting bulk; by $500, "definitely enough to get going" on small collection buys. Supplies are "not a ton." |
| ~$1,000 | One operator's phrase: "off to the races." The full loop — buy at ~70% (Chapter 2.1), list, sell, reinvest. |
| $30,000 | Not required. One operator did start with $22,000 in inventory — a lever, not a gate. |

Two rules keep the seed honest. First: no borrowed money — "you don't need credit to build
this business"; sell your personal collection and buy only what you know you can sell
(Chapter 5.2's no-leverage rule). Second: year-one earnings go back in — one operator
reinvests every dollar of first-year profit.

> **Operators disagree.** How much of the collection goes? One school is absolute: either be
> a business or don't — you can't really be both. Its example: $2,000 in the bank, $10,000 in
> a binder — sell the collection, buy inventory. The other school sells the cards that aren't
> absolute favorites and keeps "the cards that really mean a lot to you." Both agree the
> binder is the seed; they split on a sentiment carve-out. Resolve it card by card: **would
> you buy this card today, at today's price, with business money?** Yes — it's collection;
> keep it and stop counting it as business capital (Chapter 1.1's separation rule). Hesitate —
> it's inventory wearing sentiment.

## A seed, not a life raft

The tough-love caveat: one operator describes someone planning to escape financial ruin with
$200 of bulk cards — and calls the plan unrealistic. His read: someone with a bill-paying
career, passion, and willingness to work can build something significant; someone drowning
cannot. $200 is a seed for a side business, not rescue money (Chapter 1.2).

One more documented trap: for some people, the *idea* of selling cards matters more than
selling cards. The prescription on record is three verbs: get some cards, list some cards,
sell some cards. If platform choice is stalling you, Chapter 1.4's instruction is pick one
and start.

## Exercise: inventory your starting capital

On paper, tonight.

1. Pull out the binder and boxes. Comp the top items at **sold** prices, not listings
   (Chapter 2.1).
2. Make two piles: *means a lot to me* and *don't love anymore*. Use the
   would-I-buy-it-today question.
3. Total the don't-love pile at sold prices; knock off ~14% selling fees. That's
   your inventory seed.
4. Add cash you can commit without borrowing a dollar.
5. Write the number down. At $200 you're funded; at $1,000, off to the races.

## In practice

- [ ] Which of the three ingredients am I short on — and which two will pay for it?
- [ ] What's on my prerequisite list that isn't time, knowledge, or inventory?
- [ ] Is my seed number written down — sold comps, minus fees, plus cash, no debt?
- [ ] Where do my couple of hours a day come from?
- [ ] Have I listed a card yet? If not, which ingredient is missing — or is it an
      excuse from the table?

**The handle to remember:** time, knowledge, inventory. Nothing else is on the entry list.
