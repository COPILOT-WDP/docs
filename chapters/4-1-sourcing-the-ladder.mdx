One operator is offered ten collections in a bad week, twenty in a good one. One week
hit thirty-five and he halted buying — three weeks of collections were already in the mail. He
turns down nine in ten.

A newer seller has the opposite problem. The bottleneck isn't moving cards. It's finding
inventory worth selling.

## The principle

**Sourcing is a ladder, not a store.** One operator calls sourcing by far the hardest part of
reselling — there's no consistent avenue to just go buy cards. What exists is a ladder:
**your collection → bulk → collections → distribution.** Each rung up takes more capital and
delivers more inventory per hour; each rung down teaches a skill the next rung assumes. You
climb in order — every rung funds the next.

What to *pay* on any rung is [Chapter 2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy)'s job. This chapter is where the buys come from.

## The four rungs

| Rung | Capital to play | What it teaches | The catch |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Your collection** | \$0 new. A binder worth a few hundred to \$1,000 is a real seed ([Chapter 1.3](/chapters/1-3-the-three-ingredients)). | Listing, condition calls, packing, fees. | Keep 5–10 favorites. The rest is inventory now. |
| **2. Bulk** | Tens of dollars. A penny per common, ~3¢ per holo; \$10–\$20 per 1,000 cards. | Sets, card recognition, sorting systems, patience. | Slow money. Doesn't move until tens of thousands of cards are listed. |
| **3. Collections** | \$2,000–\$4,000 to compete. | Comping, negotiation, verifying at speed. | The \$300–\$500 bracket is crowded. Capital thins the crowd. |
| **4. Distribution** | Thousands per release, every release. | Allocation management, release cadence. | Hardest access in the game. A late rung, not a plan. |

**Rung 1 — your collection.** Keep the five or ten cards you love; sell the rest for capital.
The rung never retires: one operator raised about \$27,000 toward a \$100,000 collection by
selling his personal collection and vending.

**Rung 2 — bulk.** Buy, sift, resell: pick up bulk at a penny or two per card, pull the
playables and the hits, sell the remainder on — people always buy bulk. One operator found a
Latios Gold Star in a bulk lot; it sold for about \$1,100. Bulk is the school of this business —
[Chapter 3.1](/chapters/3-1-the-four-product-classes) has the economics. Caveat: competition has pushed penny bulk to 3–5¢ in some
markets.

**Rung 3 — collections.** The profit engine. One operator calls collections the most
profitable part of his business; marketplace listings and local groups are the documented
source. Capital is the filter: a \$300 collection has a line of buyers; a \$30,000 collection
has almost none. Watch for one phrase — *selling as a lot only*. A seller who won't
piece out will take a percentage.

**Rung 4 — distribution.** Its own section below.

## The list is the filter

Post that you buy collections and most of what arrives is noise — one operator estimates 90%
of inquiries are lowballs and time-wasters. The itemized list is your filter. A spreadsheet
with current values means serious. One blurry photo does not: an operator offered a flat
\$2,500 — 70% — minutes after seeing a single picture, because with no list, the bottom band is
the only offer. (What a list earns the *seller* is [Chapter 2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy)'s table.)

Run intake as a process: list first, numbers agreed before anything ships, contents verified
on arrival, payment next business day. One operation with strict written rules — sealed only,
meet at the bank or ship it, template replies — has done about \$600,000 in collection buys;
the rules turn some sellers away and make the rest seamless.

## Volume earns percentage

The 70% anchor from [Chapter 2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy) is not on sale in small quantities:

| The lot | What the buy actually looks like |
|---|---|
| One desirable card | Expect ~90%. The seller can list it themselves tonight. |
| Three or four cards | 70–75% is unrealistic — don't ask. |
| Hundreds of cards (400–2,000) | A different ballgame — percentage buying starts here. |
| "Bulk rate" on product | Means 50, 100, 150 units. Two or three doesn't qualify. |

Even the discount has a price: in a hot market, one operator could only buy 15–20% under
market by spending around \$20,000 at a time. The logic is the seller's alternative: four cards
is an evening's work; eight hundred is a month. Your percentage pays for taking that month off
their hands.

## Be the known buyer

**Deal flow is built, not found** — and visibility builds it. One operator's record shows the
whole arc: he says finding collections is easy *because* he has a YouTube channel; offers
arrive through it at about two a week — and slowed when illness paused his uploads; one viewer
brought him a \$100,000 collection; and paying 80% works, he says, because of content and
purchase scale, not the number alone. Say you're buying in every video — a
thousand viewers is a thousand reminders. [Chapter 6.2](/chapters/6-2-content-is-a-moat) covers the flywheel.

No audience yet? The local version is documented too: post repeatedly in local groups that
you're buying, say yes often enough that sellers learn you're reliable, verify fast, pay next
business day. At shows, one operator plans his table position so sellers reach him first.
People bring deals to known buyers.

## Distribution: the last rung

Distribution is buying direct from the companies that supply stores — first-wave booster boxes
run mid-\$90s to about \$106.50 while markets sit higher. The documented setup:

1. Search for card distribution sites; email them your business information and apply.
2. Once approved, set up organized play — worth three or four cases — and buy a couple
   thousand dollars of supplies to start a spend record.
3. Buy what you can responsibly afford *every* release: allocations track roughly your last
   six months of spend in the category.
4. Show up. Reps at open houses remember reliable, well-capitalized stores when discretionary
   cases appear.

Approval isn't product: one operator got his account and couldn't order a single TCG item —
his first purchase was toploaders.

> **Operators disagree.** One school builds the account early — buy every release, weak sets
> included, even selling first waves at a slight loss: one operator budgets about \$4,000 per
> release (\$4 lost on each of a thousand boxes) to stay in his distributor's good graces.
> The other school says stop planning around access: one operator
> passed half a million dollars in sales with no distributor; another puts his own odds of
> access at about 1%; a third reports spending \$3,000–\$5,000 on unwanted product can earn one
> case worth maybe \$300.
>
> Resolve it with one question: **can you spend thousands every release, on sets nobody wants,
> without needing that cash back this quarter?** If yes, the account compounds. If no, the
> middle rungs are enough — one operator flatly says buying collections you immediately list
> is no different from buying from a distributor.

## Where climbers fall

Documented failures are rung errors. Retail buying and shelf scalping are not a rung —
operators call them unsustainable and useless at scale. Pricing a seller's unlabeled pile
yourself: one operator did it, profited, and wouldn't repeat the hours. Counting on one source:
an operator drove to a show to buy sealed and failed; at other shows, buyers paid 100–110% of
market to leave with product. Tying up every dollar: a deal that needs
\$20,000 in cash walks past ([Chapter 2.3](/chapters/2-3-cashflow-is-oxygen)). There is no infinite supply of collections. The
ladder rewards climbers, not campers.

## In practice

- [ ] Which rung am I on, and does my bankroll cover it (\$2,000–\$4,000 for collections;
      [Chapter 5.2](/chapters/5-2-bankroll-discipline))?
- [ ] Do I require an itemized list before I comp anything? No list, bottom band — or pass.
- [ ] Does my offer respect volume, or am I asking a 70% price on a four-card lot?
- [ ] Do sellers know I'm buying? When did I last say so — post, video, table?
- [ ] Am I planning around distributor access I don't have?

**The handle to remember:** sourcing is a ladder, not a store — each rung funds the next, and
the deals go to known buyers.
