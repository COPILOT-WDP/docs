An operator with a growing store ran a pricing experiment without meaning to. For weeks he
offered sellers 78% of market value for their collections. Silence. Nobody bit. He raised his
number to 80% — and the messages started arriving. Same product, same market, two points more.

He wasn't spending those two points on cards. He was buying deal flow.

This chapter is about the most important number in your business: the price you pay. Not the
price you sell at — the price you pay.

## The principle

**Money is made on the buy.** The day you acquire inventory, your margin is decided. Everything
that happens afterward — listing, pricing, shipping — is execution. Execution can protect a
margin. It cannot create one.

Here's why. Your selling price is not really yours to choose: the market sets it, and if you
price above it, your inventory sits. Your costs are not yours to choose either: platform fees,
shipping, taxes, and supplies don't negotiate ([Chapter 2.2](/chapters/2-2-the-real-cost-stack) itemizes them). The only number in
the whole chain you fully control is the one on the front end.

Overpay, and no amount of hustle downstream fixes it. Buy right, and you can make mistakes
everywhere else and still come out ahead.

## The arithmetic

Take a collection with a market value of \$1,000 — meaning \$1,000 at *recently sold* prices, not
sticker prices. You'll sell it at 95% of market to keep it moving ([Chapter 2.4](/chapters/2-4-selling-is-a-math-problem) covers why). What
you keep depends entirely on what you paid:

| You pay | At a lean cost stack (~8%) | At a typical cost stack (~15%) |
|---|---|---|
| **70% — \$700** | **+\$174** (25% on your money) | **+\$108** (15%) |
| 75% — \$750 | +\$124 (17%) | +\$58 (8%) |
| 80% — \$800 | +\$74 (9%) | +\$8 (1%) |
| 85% — \$850 | +\$24 (3%) | **−\$43 (a loss)** |

*Gross: \$950. "Cost stack" = every selling cost as a share of gross — fees, shipping, supplies.
Where you land in the 8–15% range depends on your venue and volume; see [Chapter 2.2](/chapters/2-2-the-real-cost-stack).*

Read the bottom row again. At an 85% buy on a typical cost stack, you did all the work —
sorting, photographing, listing, packing, customer service — to lose \$43. That's not a card
business. That's a hobby with extra steps.

And notice what the table really says: **your cost stack decides which buy price you can
survive.** An operator with a lean, high-volume setup can pay 80% and live. A beginner paying
retail fees cannot. Never copy someone's buy price without copying their cost stack.

## The 70% anchor

Across hundreds of documented deals, the same default keeps appearing: **offer about 70% of
market value for a collection.** It's not a magic number. It's the number that leaves room for
a 5% discount to sell fast, up to 15% in selling costs, your hours, and a mistake or two — and
still pays you.

Treat 70% as the anchor, then adjust deliberately:

| Situation | Your band | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Standard collection buy | **~70%** | The default. It prices in your costs, your time, and your risk. |
| You're new and learning | up to 75% | Acceptable tuition. Volume matters more than perfection early. |
| Itemized, clean, liquid inventory | 75–80% | The seller did hours of your work. Two or three extra points for a full itemized list is a trade, not a gift. |
| Hot product you *know* turns | 80%+ | Only with proven velocity and a lean cost stack. This is an expert move that looks easy. |
| Slow sets, ancillary product | Dirt cheap — or pass | If it sits, every point you paid becomes storage rent. One operator calls paying 80% for ancillary product one of his stupidest decisions. |
| Buying from another vendor | Don't lead with 70% | Vendor-to-vendor trades near 90%+. Offering a dealer collector prices is how you end a relationship. |
| Bulk | Different math entirely | Bulk runs on cents-per-card and labor, not percentages. See [Chapter 3.1](/chapters/3-1-the-four-product-classes). |

The bands move. The logic never does: **the buy price must survive your cost stack, your time,
and your sell-through discount — with profit left over.**

## Two points buy the pipeline

> **Operators disagree.** One school says discipline is everything: hold the line at 70%, let
> the impatient overpay, profit from their mistakes later. Another school pays 80% all day for
> the right product and wins on volume: at 78% the phone was silent; at 80% it rang, and a full
> pipeline at a thin margin beat an empty one at a fat margin.
>
> Both are right — about their own operations. The high payer runs proven velocity and a lean
> stack; every extra point he pays is repaid by turn speed. The discipline school protects
> beginners, who have neither. Resolve it with one question: **does this buy price keep my
> inventory turning at a margin my cost stack survives?** If you can't answer with numbers,
> you're in the discipline school — for now.

An empty inbox is information. So is a full one. If nobody accepts your offers for a month,
your number isn't disciplined — it's dead. If everyone accepts instantly, you're the most
generous buyer in town, and that's a title you pay for.

## Know the price before you talk

Three rules cover most of negotiation:

1. **Market value means sold prices.** What something is *listed* for is a wish. What it
   *sold* for this month is a fact. Comp the top items in any lot before you offer — always
   from sold data.
2. **Let the seller go first.** You don't have to play both buyer and seller. Ask what they
   think it's worth. Their number tells you whether a deal is even possible before you've
   revealed anything.
3. **Itemized lists filter serious sellers.** Most inquiries — one operator estimates 90% —
   are lowballs, flakes, and window-shoppers. Someone who shows up with a spreadsheet and
   current values is real, saved you hours, and has earned a better band. Price the courtesy in.

Scale changes the stakes, not the rules. On a \$100,000 collection, every 5% of negotiation is
\$5,000, and the weeks you'll spend sorting and listing it are a real cost. Big lots deserve
sharper pencils, not looser ones.

## When you get it wrong

You will overpay. Documented operators with hundreds of thousands in sales still tell stories
about the box bought at \$700 that was worth \$720 — all that work to break even — and the \$900
boxes that sat unsold in their stores for months. The difference between them and the sellers
who quit is what happened next: they wrote the number down, figured out which band they broke,
and adjusted.

Early on, treat small losses as tuition. A bad buy is a lesson with a receipt. A *pattern* of
bad buys is a system problem — it means you're comping from listed prices, skipping the cost
stack, or letting the thrill of the deal set your band. The market will keep teaching the same
lesson at increasing prices until you learn it.

You don't need every lot to be profitable in your first months. You need every lot to make you
a sharper buyer.

## In practice

Before any offer, you should be able to answer all six:

- [ ] What did the key items in this lot **sell** for recently? (Sold comps, not listings.)
- [ ] What's my all-in cost stack on my venue? ([Chapter 2.2](/chapters/2-2-the-real-cost-stack) — if you don't know it, stop here.)
- [ ] Which band does this inventory belong in — and am I adjusting for a reason, or a feeling?
- [ ] Will this turn in weeks, not months? Priced to sit is priced to lose.
- [ ] Have I priced my own hours? Sorting a chaos lot is work; an itemized list is worth points.
- [ ] Can I afford to be wrong on this one? (If it's rent money, the answer is no — [Chapter 5.2](/chapters/5-2-bankroll-discipline).)

**The handle to remember:** you make money when you buy. Everything else is collecting it.
