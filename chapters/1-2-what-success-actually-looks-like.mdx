One operator published a complete breakdown of his first month selling cards online: \$1,481 in
sales. 83 orders. \$1,249 left after fees. It wasn't even a full month — he started around May 12,
so call it two-thirds of one. Counted honestly, he says, the month might even have been a loss,
because he didn't know the true cost of everything he sold. He put the \$1,249 back into inventory
and kept going.

Two years later, that same evening side project had done close to \$450,000 in sales.

Both halves of that story are true, and you need both. One without the other is either despair
or a sales pitch.

## The principle

**Breaking even while learning is success.** Your first months are not a payday; they're **the
tuition period** — a paid-in-margin education in buying, negotiating, listing, sorting,
condition-grading, and knowing what sells versus what sits. One operator calls the early phase
"working for free tuition." Another says being a couple hundred dollars up after a month or two
of real work is normal. A third was content to break even or even lose a little in month one,
because he was buying long-term standing on his selling platform, not groceries.

Why is break-even the honest benchmark? Because early on you are slow and wrong at every step
that protects margin. The operator in the cold open missed "a lot of money" in his first month
by pricing cards too low. His logs also include \$800 lost on boxes that dropped before they
sold, and a collection buy that "maybe broke even, maybe made a couple grand" — he can't say
which, because prices moved while he sold it and profit tracking was still a skill in progress.
The market charges for these lessons whether or not you budget for them. Budget for them.

## Two documented trajectories

Here is what the curve actually looks like, from two operators who published their numbers as
they went. These are *sales*, not profit — keep that distinction loaded; [Chapter 2.4](/chapters/2-4-selling-is-a-math-problem) fires it.

| Checkpoint | Operator A — evenings, seeded from his own collection | Operator B — nights around a 60–75-hour/week job, \$22,000 of starting inventory |
|---|---|---|
| Month 1 | \$1,481 sales · 83 orders · \$1,249 after fees · ≈ zero profit | \$14,858 sales in an October launch month |
| Months 2–3 | 577 orders · \$3,267 sales · \$5.66 average order · ≈ \$490 profit at his 15% estimate | ≈ \$49,000 cumulative by New Year |
| Months 4–9 | ≈ nine months to pass \$100,000 cumulative | ≈ \$55,000 in January alone; \$423,947 and 1,017 orders by month eight — ≈ \$50,873 profit at his 12% estimate |
| ~Two years | ≈ \$450,000 cumulative · \$2,500–\$4,000 profit per month · still ~3 evening hours a day | still employed; one month's profit hit ≈ \$15,000 — double his paycheck — and he plans to keep the job for three more years |

Read the profit cells, not the sales cells. A 577-order month — packing almost 20 orders a day —
earned Operator A about \$490. Month eight put Operator B's estimated profit around \$50,873 on
\$423,947 in sales: roughly a salary, earned on top of a 60-hour-a-week career. Margins in this
business run thin — these two operators estimate 12% and 15% — so revenue headlines flatter
everyone. And the curve isn't smooth: one operator sketches a typical path as \$300 in month one,
then \$800, then \$2,000, then back down to \$1,600. Growth that stalls and resumes is still
growth. **Success is a slope, not a spike.**

Two honesty notes. Operator B started with \$22,000 of inventory and a hot market at his back —
he says himself that the longer the boom runs, the more he wants to keep his job. And the
ceiling case exists: one operator documented building from a laptop in a bedroom to roughly
\$15 million in sales over five years. Even he ran the card business alongside his real-estate
job for months, shipping orders after work, and only stepped away when the profits made staying
illogical. The biggest documented outcome in the evidence base started exactly like the smallest.

## Nearly everyone starts part-time

One operator's estimate: **99% of people who start a Pokemon card business start it as a side
hustle.** His stronger claim: you don't get to skip that step — you *have* to start part-time,
because the business can't pay you a reliable wage before it exists.

The documented lives behind the table above: a full-time pastor with three kids, listing cards
after bedtime. A 60-to-75-hour-a-week career, with orders packed at night. A real-estate job,
with shipping runs after work. None of this is passive income — operators are blunt that
selling cards is active work — and it is explicitly okay for the business to stay a side hustle
for a significant period of time. Set boundaries so it doesn't damage the job and family that
are funding it. What you need to start is smaller than you think — [Chapter 1.3](/chapters/1-3-the-three-ingredients) prices a real
start at \$200 to \$1,000 — and [Chapter 1.4](/chapters/1-4-validate-before-you-build) gives you the validation gate to pass before you
spend anything on scale.

What about shortcuts? When a four-figure course promising this exact business made the rounds,
the documented operator reaction was arithmetic, not outrage: that price is about two weeks of
an average paycheck, and you won't get there on anything a course teaches — spend that money's
worth of hours doing the work instead. The record is just as blunt about the fantasy
underneath: you don't go from no money to rich here; the escape-from-financial-ruin stories are
a minority; \$200 of bulk will not fix a broken financial situation; "it's not a get-rich-quick
scheme." Selling thousands of dollars in your first month is a documented example of an
unrealistic expectation. You now know what a real first month looks like, to the dollar.

> **Operators disagree.** When does full-time enter the picture? One school treats it as the
> natural next rung: run the business beside the job until the profits make staying illogical,
> then step across — plenty of store owners have made that transition. The other school treats
> it as a distant threshold: don't even discuss it until the business has *replaced* your job
> income, with six to twelve months of bills banked. One operator whose monthly profit hit
> double his paycheck is keeping his job for three more years anyway; another, near half a
> million in sales, says attempt full-time only when the business is going very well or the job
> is truly unacceptable. Resolve it with one question: **has the business already replaced your
> income — through a slow season — with reserves left over?** If you have to ask, you're in
> side-hustle school for now. [Chapter 6.3](/chapters/6-3-going-full-time-or-not) holds the full framework.

## In practice

Before you start — or before you judge your own month one — answer these:

- [ ] What number would make month one a success? (If it isn't "roughly zero, plus an
      education," reread this chapter.)
- [ ] Do you have 5–10 evening hours a week that your job and family can actually spare — with
      boundaries?
- [ ] Is your starting money money you can leave inside the business for a year? ([Chapters 1.3](/chapters/1-3-the-three-ingredients)
      and 5.2.)
- [ ] Are you tracking profit, or quoting sales? The documented margins here are 12–15%.
- [ ] If month four comes in below month three, what's your plan besides quitting?
- [ ] Is success, to you, a trajectory — learn, systemize, reinvest — or a date you quit your
      job? Only one of those is in your control.

**The handle to remember:** the first months are the tuition period. Breaking even while
learning isn't failing at the business — it's the price of admission, and everyone pays it.
