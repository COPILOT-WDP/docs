One operator put \$30,000 into a single Japanese set — almost all his working capital. Then a
distributor invoice came due, and the only money he had was sealed inside those boxes. He paid
the bill by selling the set at a loss. Years later he watched a better-capitalized store buy
sealed cases week after week and told his audience it works for that operator — real money,
business experience, a funnel that moves product as fast as it arrives — then added: don't
copy it, "because you could get absolutely burned."

Same class. Same market. One bankroll makes it a strategy. The other makes it a countdown.

## The principle

[Chapter 3.1](/chapters/3-1-the-four-product-classes) profiled the four product classes. This one is about which you've earned the right
to play. **Sealed is a capital game. Bulk is a time game.** Every class converts one resource
into margin — bulk converts hours, sealed converts capital, singles convert knowledge plus
labor, slabs convert judgment plus waiting. Feed one the resource you're short of and the
machine seizes: locked cash on one side, dead hours on the other.

## The bankroll rule

One documented rule for undercapitalized sellers is blunt: stay out of the sealed game
entirely — stick to singles — "because you're going to have funds locked up." Sealed money
leaves in \$400 boxes and \$2,000 cases and comes back only when a big-ticket item sells. The
same operator describes slow sealed product in his own store as "locking up funds into things
that simply are just illiquid." And he *is* capitalized.

| Deployable bankroll | Where you can play | Documented reality at this level |
|---|---|---|
| **\$200–\$500** | Bulk, plus singles from your own binder | Bulk entry runs \$20–\$100. At \$200–\$300 you're sifting bulk — collection buying isn't available yet. |
| **\$500–\$2,000** | Small singles lots | A documented \$510-market lot went at 70% (\$357). The most competition lives here: every reseller can afford these deals. |
| **\$2,000–\$5,000** | Collections at ~70% become realistic | \$2,000–\$3,000 gives a real chance at a few hundred cards at 70%; buys this size start to move a business. |
| **Five figures** | Large collections; sealed as a class | Lower-five-figure collections "pretty easily"; \$50,000–\$60,000 plays possible. Less competition — and sealed stops being a countdown. |

Two riders. The floor is not a ban: the documented scaling rule is "buy one booster box if
that's all you can afford," paired with its risk-side twin — only spend what you can truly
afford to hold. And [Chapter 5.2](/chapters/5-2-bankroll-discipline) governs the bankroll itself: no debt, reserves liquid.
Liquidity is offense too — one operator warns that with all your cash tied up in inventory,
you miss the 80%-of-market collection that needs \$20,000 cash this week. Locked capital costs
you the next buy, not just the next invoice.

## Time-rich or cash-rich: pick your currency

**The time-rich path (bulk → singles).** Bulk's entry ticket is \$20 to \$100 — the last cheap
thing about it. Operators call it "a massive time suck": consistent bulk sales take
20,000–40,000 cards listed, and one documented store has someone sorting bulk all day, every
day. The real product is an education: bulk forces you to learn sets, conditions, and your own
listing systems, because you have to list everything. The documented advice for low-capital
beginners: learn bulk before buying collections; use it to build the account for bigger shots.
But the ceiling is real too: bulk builds "some capital, not a ton," and it will not rescue you
from a horrible financial situation. A school with a stipend, not a salary.

**The cash-rich path (collections → sealed → slabs).** Collection flipping moves faster with
fewer cards — but takes the capital up front. Deployable capital is a documented unfair
advantage: the more you can spend, the less competition you face. Sealed compresses effort per
dollar — selling by the case means fewer buyers and faster exits at a slight discount — but it
demands two things: a channel that captures the value (a sealed deal at 75% of market is a good
pickup *only* with rip-and-ship, a site, a stream, or singles channels behind it), and the
temperament to sit; operators who play sealed well will hold cases for years if necessary.
Slabs sit at the far end (3.1 has the grading math): capital in a queue, with documented
bulk-submission turnarounds near nine months.

| | Time-rich path | Cash-rich path |
|---|---|---|
| **Classes** | Bulk → singles | Collections → sealed → slabs |
| **Entry ticket** | \$20–\$100 | \$2,000+ per deal; five figures for sealed depth |
| **What it teaches** | Sets, conditions, listing systems | Comping, negotiation, market judgment |
| **What kills it** | The time suck: tens of thousands of listings before sales are consistent | Locked cash: the invoice you can't pay, the deal you can't take |
| **Temperament test** | Can you sort and list every evening for a year? | Can you let a five-figure position sit for months without needing the cash? |

The classes are also a curriculum, and you graduate in order — bulk teaches systems; singles
and small collections teach comps, condition calls, and [Chapter 2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy)'s bands; sealed tests
judgment and channel strength; slabs test patience. [Chapter 4.1](/chapters/4-1-sourcing-the-ladder)'s ladder climbs the same rungs,
and no tool skips a grade: a sorting machine won't make you competent at a game you never
learned.

## One lane, run deep

Growth changes the question from "which class can I afford?" to "which class am I?" The
documented rule: **as a business grows, focus on one core lane** — selling bulk, buying and
selling collections, consignment, grading — **and say no to the rest.** With two evening hours,
anything you add is subtracted from the lane that pays. When a hot new card game tempted one
operator, his documented test was a single question: is this diversification serving the
business or distracting it from its lane? He kept his head down.

Mixing classes is fine; documented operators do it constantly. The difference is design:

- **A core lane plus a filler.** The documented hybrid: collections at ~70% as the main
  engine; in the downtime between deals, list bulk. Not a second business — it monetizes idle
  hours.
- **A reserve with a rule.** One operator sits on sealed only when enough capital remains to
  keep the singles side running — the savings account, never the checking.
- **Drift looks different.** Four half-lanes, none systematized, cash locked in all of them —
  classes you're "in" only because they arrived inside a collection and never left. If you
  can't say what job a class does, it doesn't have one.

**Mix by design, not by drift.**

> **Operators disagree.** Where should the first \$3,000–\$5,000 go? One school says into the
> machine: buy collections at ~70%, list bulk in the gaps, keep cash cycling — and stay out of
> sealed until you're capitalized, because sealed locks funds. The other — one operator's
> bull-market answer, wrapped in heavy not-advice disclaimers — buys the hot, best-of-the-best
> sets instead of cheap slow sellers: even a stretched bankroll must stay in things that move.
> Both are velocity arguments ([Chapter 2.3](/chapters/2-3-cashflow-is-oxygen)); they differ on which risk is worse — margin locked
> in a box, or hours locked in slow inventory. Resolve it with two questions. *If this doesn't
> sell for six months, is my business still alive?* And *if it does sell, do I have a channel
> that captures the margin?* Two yeses, and you can consider the hot-product school. Either no,
> and you're in singles and bulk — for now.

## In practice

- [ ] What's your deployable bankroll — cash you can lock in inventory without touching rent
  money? ([Chapter 5.2](/chapters/5-2-bankroll-discipline) comes first.)
- [ ] Which do you have more of, hours or dollars? Does your main class spend that one?
- [ ] Before any sealed buy: can you afford to hold it, and do you have a channel that captures
  it?
- [ ] Can you name your core lane in one sentence? If you need three, you have zero.
- [ ] For each class you touch: what job does it do — engine, filler, or reserve — and what is
  it supposed to be teaching you?

**The handle to remember:** sealed is a capital game, bulk is a time game — play the one your
bankroll can survive, and run one lane deep.
