One operator's backlog holds about 300,000 unprocessed bulk cards, and he calls bulk the
easiest inventory he can get. Another won't touch it: an elite trainer box (ETB), he
figures, makes him \$5 much faster than forty or fifty bulk cards would. Both are
profitable. Neither is wrong. They aren't running different math — they're trading different
products.

## The principle

**Four products, one engine.** Everything in Part 2 holds no matter what you sell: margin is
locked on the buy ([Chapter 2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy)), the cost stack takes its cut ([2.2](/chapters/2-2-the-real-cost-stack)), cash has to keep moving
([2.3](/chapters/2-3-cashflow-is-oxygen)), price buys liquidity ([2.4](/chapters/2-4-selling-is-a-math-problem)). What a product class changes is the *parameters* — spread,
turn speed, capital per unit, labor per dollar.

The parameters aren't random. Line up the documented buy prices — bulk for pennies on the
dollar, singles collections around 70% of market, liquid sealed near 80%, slabs at 85–90%
— and the pattern names itself: **the spread follows the work.** The more sorting,
condition-calling, grading, and trust a product still needs, the deeper your discount, because
you supply those things. A slab needs nothing. It's nearly cash, and priced like it.

## Singles: paid to make the condition call

Singles are granular and steady: one card at a time, near-mint sells fast, and anything from
about 50¢ up sells at market price. The margin model is [Chapter 2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy)'s — buy around 70%, sell
5% under market, pay ~5% in fees, keep 15–20%. Entry is cheap — one documented first flip was
a \$70 card sold at \$130 for about \$25 after fees — and so is fulfillment: penny sleeve, top
loader, plain white envelope (PWE), under a dollar in postage.

The tax is effort per dollar. Every card is its own sort, condition call, listing, and pick.
One operator had \$3,000 to \$15,000 in singles sitting unlisted on his office floor
because listing was the bottleneck — and only listed inventory sells ([Chapter 4.3](/chapters/4-3-listing-and-inventory-discipline)). The skill
here is **condition knowledge**: condition standards look objective on paper, but customers
dispute your calls, and platforms tend to side with them. Your condition call is your
reputation, and your margin.

## Sealed: capital in a box

Sealed is the easiest listing in the business: one SKU, no per-card condition call — one
operator listed a \$7,500 sealed collection in about twenty minutes. The price is
concentration: boxes run hundreds, cases thousands — every buy parks your bankroll in a few
chunky pieces. Hot sealed is the fastest money in the hobby — one store's hot ETBs sold the
same day listed, sometimes within the hour. Cold sealed sits:
documented boxes took weeks, some two months, and the \$900 boxes from [Chapter 2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy) sat unsold.
Liquidity thins as tickets rise: documented slowdown at \$600–800, a smaller buyer bracket past
\$500. The best cost bases come from distributor allocation — documented near \$35 an ETB — the
ladder's top rung ([Chapter 4.1](/chapters/4-1-sourcing-the-ladder)), not where you start.

Four risks are signature sealed:

1. **Capital lockup.** The documented rule for undercapitalized sellers: stay out of the
   sealed game; it locks funds into illiquid pieces. You can sell half a binder — not half a
   booster box.
2. **The case premium.** A sealed case trades roughly 15–30% above the same product loose, and
   condition applies at the box level: loose wrappers knocked one buy from 80% to 75%, stacked
   ETBs crease the bottom units, and opening a case to sell one unit erases the premium on the
   rest.
3. **Reprints.** Loose ETBs are documented as most exposed to reprint waves and big-box
   restocks; sealed cases less so ([Chapter 5.3](/chapters/5-3-the-risk-map)).
4. **The hold temptation.** Sealed sometimes appreciates, so it whispers *hold me*. But
   appreciation is not profit until you sell: one operator keeps \$45,000 of sealed because, he
   admits, investing is fun; another planned five-to-ten-year holds and sold early —
   opportunity cost. Hold only what your operating cash never needs (Chapters [2.3](/chapters/2-3-cashflow-is-oxygen), [5.2](/chapters/5-2-bankroll-discipline)).

## Bulk: the school of the business

Bulk is the cheapest door into the building and the most expensive room to stand in — priced
in hours. Commons cost about a penny, holos 3–4¢; \$20 to \$100 buys thousands of cards to sift,
pull, and list. The percentages are absurd — one documented experiment turned \$3 of three-cent
holos into about \$22 of profit — but the dollars are small: bulk-only sellers documented at
\$10,000–\$40,000 a year in sales, with grinding. "Can you get rich selling bulk? Probably not."

The labor is the business. "Bulk is a massive time suck" — from an operator who *champions*
it; one retail store staffs its bulk-sorting room all day, every day. And bulk has
a threshold: 4,000–5,000 cards listed gets an order or two a day; consistent sales start in
the tens of thousands of listings, and nobody can name the exact point. The working rule when
sales stall: *list more*.

So why start here? Because bulk is **the school of the business**. Thousands of cards through
your hands teach sets, playable cards, condition, and listing systems — with almost no capital
at risk, since sifted leftovers resell for about \$10 per thousand. "You need to learn how to
do bulk before you buy collections," as one operator put it. The one way to break bulk's
near-risk-free math is buying machinery — one documented sorting machine "did not end up
working out." Machines amplify working systems; they rescue nothing.

> **Operators disagree.** Is bulk worth doing at all? One school calls bulk margins "through
> the roof," near risk-free, the easiest inventory to source. The other school skips it: an
> ETB makes \$5 far faster than fifty bulk cards, and even bulk's champions concede the time
> suck and the modest ceiling. Both are answering with different scarcities. Resolve it with
> one question: **which do you have more of — hours or dollars?** Time-rich, bulk pays you to
> learn. Cash-rich, your hour is worth more in another class ([Chapter 3.2](/chapters/3-2-matching-class-to-capital)).

## Slabs: nearly cash, priced like it

A slab is a card a grading company has authenticated, graded, and encased — the work is
already done, certified, and priced in. Documented show buyers offer 85% booth-wide;
one team bought any slab under \$200 at 90%; dealers buying at 80–85% resold at 87–90%. The
spread is 3–10 points, not 30, made up in ticket size: the lowest effort per dollar of the
four classes, the highest capital per unit. Liquidity is lumpy: one operator
moved four slabs in a whole show weekend; a documented six-figure slab lot posted locally was
spoken for within hours.

Making slabs — grading raw cards — carries four documented risks:

1. **Fee versus uplift.** Grading runs from under \$20 at budget graders to an \$80 minimum at
   the dominant company (once \$25). The graded price must clear the raw price, the fee, and
   your capital's downtime.
2. **Grade-outcome risk.** In one documented set play, "the only way you basically make money
   there is if you get tens." Pre-grade estimates are explicitly not guarantees.
3. **Turnaround is capital lockup.** Documented waits: 40–50 business days at the \$80 tier,
   three to five months standard, about nine months for bulk submissions. One operator's
   verdict on buy-raw-and-grade: "that's dead" for most cards — the market moves before your
   cards come home.
4. **Buyers have grade preferences.** One vendor's show policy: "not gonna take anything but
   PSA" — it's what his customers ask for — and he never pays top-label prices for other
   companies' slabs because they're harder to offload. A slab is worth what its label's buyer
   pool pays.

## The comparison matrix

Four parameter sets, one table to come back to:

| Class | Margin profile | Velocity & liquidity | Capital to enter | Effort per dollar | Storage & fulfillment | Signature risks |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Singles** | Buy ~70%, keep ~15–20% | Steady, granular; near-mint sells fast | Low — starts under \$100 | High — every card is a condition call and a listing | Set-sorted boxes; ships PWE under \$1 | Labor per dollar; condition disputes settled in the buyer's favor |
| **Sealed** | Buy ~75–80% (liquid); thin, volume-driven; case premium adds 15–30% | Chunky — minutes when hot, weeks-to-months when not; slows past ~\$500–800 | High — boxes \$100s, cases \$1,000s | Lowest to list — one SKU, \$7,500 in ~20 minutes | Real shelf space; crush/dent risk; ~\$7 to ship an ETB | Capital lockup; reprints; box-level condition; the hold temptation |
| **Bulk** | Pennies in — huge percentages, small dollars | Slow until tens of thousands are listed | Lowest — \$20–100 | Highest — sift, sort, list by the thousand | Clogs storage and picking lanes; ships cheap | The labor sink; the listing threshold; machine spending breaks the risk-free math |
| **Slabs** | Buy 85–90% — a 3–10 point spread on big tickets | Deep at the right price, lumpy at shows | Highest per unit | Low — pre-graded, comp-priced, one listing | Display case; nightly lock-up; boxed shipping | Grading fee vs. uplift; turnaround lockup; grade outcome; label preference (PSA-first buyers) |

Read the columns, not just the rows: margin and effort rise together — the spread following
the work.

## In practice

Before the next dollar goes into any class, answer all six:

- [ ] Which class holds most of my money right now — did I choose it, or drift into it?
- [ ] Can my bankroll survive it? Sealed and slabs park hundreds or thousands in pieces you
      can't partially sell ([Chapter 5.2](/chapters/5-2-bankroll-discipline)).
- [ ] Can my calendar survive it? Bulk and singles pay labor rates before they pay margins.
- [ ] Can I make the condition call — per card for singles, per box and case for sealed?
- [ ] If grading: does the uplift clear the fee, the raw cost, months of lockup — and who buys
      this label when it comes back?
- [ ] Does my selling venue actually fit this class ([Chapter 4.2](/chapters/4-2-choosing-where-to-sell))?

**The handle to remember:** four products, one engine. Pick the parameters your money and your
hours can survive.
