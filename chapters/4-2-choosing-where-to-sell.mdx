One operator's best vending day ever grossed \$6,200. Then he ran the math nobody at the show
runs: fifteen to twenty hours repricing and stickering cards beforehand, an hour or two of
driving each way, eight hours at the table. Twenty-five to thirty hours, all-in, for one
Saturday. His online store does \$6,000 "pretty much every Saturday" — whether he's standing
anywhere or not.

Same cards. Same market. The venue didn't change what he sold; it changed what his hour was
worth.

## The principle

**Every venue is a traffic deal: you rent buyers, or you bring your own.** A marketplace takes
10–15% of every sale because it delivers the customer. Your own website takes about 3% because
it delivers nobody. A live stream pays you in speed and followers and bills you in hours on
camera. A card show hands you cash at the table and quietly invoices you a 25-hour day.

There is no best venue. There is a best venue for your inventory, your audience, and your hour
— and a right order to enter them.

## The four archetypes

**Marketplaces (TCGplayer, eBay).** Demand is built in: list 1,000 sought-after cards at
market price and one operator documents 60–80 orders by the weekend. You pay for that
delivery — roughly 10–15% of every sale. The two big venues aren't interchangeable: one is
built for listing everything, floor-priced commons at 20–25 cents included; the other is where
vintage sells faster and for more money. Match the venue to your product class ([Chapter 3.1](/chapters/3-1-the-four-product-classes)).

Marketplaces also pay you back for showing up well. On one, about 50 orders with good feedback
unlocks "level four": a free-shipping threshold that makes bulk viable, bulk listing tools,
and a seller storefront running ~5% total fees instead of 15. The monthly statement from
[Chapter 2.2](/chapters/2-2-the-real-cost-stack) shows the split: \$17,914 through his storefront cost \$987 in fees; \$7,412 through
the open marketplace cost \$1,123. The documented play: speed-run those 50 orders with desirable cards;
don't attempt bulk before the perks exist.

**Your own website (Shopify/Wix-class).** The economics invert: about \$30 a month fixed,
roughly 2.5–3% processing, and the rest of the fee stack disappears. You own the customer —
names, emails, addresses — and one operator's explicit plan, if his platform sours, is to take
the list and move. The documented catch: list \$100 of inventory on a website nobody knows
about, and nobody buys it, because nobody knows about it. The rule from an operator with
15,000+ marketplace transactions: **a standalone website is not a first channel unless you
already have an audience in the space.** Traffic is the real product a marketplace sells you.
On your own site, you bring it — usually with content ([Chapter 6.2](/chapters/6-2-content-is-a-moat)).

**Live and social selling (Whatnot-class).** The fastest venue and the hungriest. Velocity is
real — operators use quick live sales to build capital — and the room often pays at or above
market: packs that list at \$23–24 clear around \$26 on streams, and stream sellers can
reportedly pay 90–100% on the buy because of it. Now the bill. One
documented operation runs weekly rip-and-ship nights grossing \$1,700–\$2,900 — about \$425 a
night at a 17% margin — for two and a half to three hours live, plus prep, every week. You
only sell while you're on camera. And the rent can be raised mid-lease: one social
marketplace's seller fee went from 2% to 6% to 13% inside its early years. Renting doesn't get
more rented than that.

**In-person (shows, local marketplaces).** The margins look beautiful at the table: cash or
~2.5% card processing, same-day money, buyers who are less picky — B-grade sealed that online
buyers reject can bring full market in person. Then the fully-loaded hour arrives: 25–30 hours
for a one-day show, table fees from \$50 to \$300, buyers negotiating a \$500 item down to 87–90%
before you've covered the table, and one operator's helper-less ten-hour day penciling out near
\$70 an hour before inventory cost. Demand is venue-specific too: one operator's 151 bundles
sold instantly online at \$110 and not at all at his show. Local marketplaces add the **flake
tax** — flakes, no-shows, and people who try to shortchange you after you've driven out to
meet them. And a show-only business doesn't scale: you're renting someone else's table,
someone else's marketing, someone else's crowd.

## The trade-off matrix

| | Marketplaces | Own website | Live/social | In-person |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Traffic source** | Platform's buyers | Yours to bring | Feed + your following | The show's foot traffic |
| **Cost shape** | ~10–15% per sale | ~\$30/mo + ~3% | Commission — fee creep documented | Table fee + ~0–2.5% |
| **Effort per sale** | Low once listed | Low per sale, high to earn the visit | High — you sell only while live | Highest — the 25–30-hour day |
| **Payout speed** | Delayed cycles | Fast — until it isn't ([5.3](/chapters/5-3-the-risk-map)) | Platform-controlled | Same-day cash |
| **Customer data** | Platform's | Yours | Platform's | Nobody's, unless you collect it |
| **Deplatforming risk** | Real | Lower, not zero ([5.3](/chapters/5-3-the-risk-map)) | Real, plus fee changes | Shows set rules too — one banned retail sealed outright |
| **Scale ceiling** | High | High, capped by your audience | Capped by live hours | Low — weekends and driving distance |

## Renting first, owning second

1. **Start where the buyers already are.** For a new seller, the documented step one is
   choosing between the two big marketplaces — not building a storefront. Renting demand is
   how you validate ([Chapter 1.4](/chapters/1-4-validate-before-you-build)) without paying to be invisible.
2. **One channel, mastered.** Pick one platform and become an expert in it — its shipping, its
   policies, its terms — before branching. Operators call branching out unwise until the first
   platform is completely mastered.
3. **If you're stuck choosing, flip a coin.** Documented advice, nearly verbatim: "literally
   flip a coin, pick one and get going." The platform you pick won't be the thing that holds
   you back. Not picking will.
4. **Own the customer once you've earned one.** When a real audience exists — repeat buyers, a
   following, an email list — the ~3% channel starts beating the 15% channel. The end state is
   platform-agnostic: buyers who follow *you*, not the venue. One documented brand moves
   across four platforms because its customers follow the seller.

> **Operators disagree.** One school says build your own website as soon as possible — one
> operator built his before his first \$1,000, points at the \$130 in fees a marketplace charged
> on that first \$1,000, and treats the email list as the whole point. The other school says
> the standalone site comes last: without an audience it's a store on a road nobody drives —
> and mostly-singles inventory makes it the wrong first move entirely.
> Both schools agree on the destination — own the customer eventually. They disagree on when
> the toll is worth paying. Resolve it with one question: **how many people would visit your
> store this week if no marketplace sent them?** If that's a real number, build. If it's a
> shrug, keep renting.

## When the venue turns on you

One operator was on pace for a \$100,000 month — thousands of dollars a day — when the email
arrived: store shut down over a copyright claim on the website *theme* he had paid about \$350
for. Orders froze. He believed the platform was holding \$20,000–22,000 he couldn't even log in
to verify. He spent the next days pulling receipts to prove his own inventory was
authentic.

The full story, and the rest of the risk map, is [Chapter 5.3](/chapters/5-3-the-risk-map). The venue lesson lives here:
**even the "owned" channel is rented at the platform layer.** Keep your customer data
exportable and a second channel warm enough to restart on. The fee that matters most is the
one a venue can charge you all at once.

## In practice

Before you commit a quarter to a venue:

- [ ] Where does my product class already sell — list-everything singles, vintage, sealed,
      bulk? ([Chapter 3.1](/chapters/3-1-the-four-product-classes).)
- [ ] Am I renting traffic or bringing it? If bringing: how many people, counted, will show up
      this week?
- [ ] What is my fully-loaded hour — prep, travel, and table time, not just the fee?
- [ ] Does the payout speed fit my cash position? ([Chapter 2.3](/chapters/2-3-cashflow-is-oxygen) — cash flow is oxygen.)
- [ ] Am I mastering one channel, or dabbling in three?
- [ ] If this venue emailed me a shutdown notice tomorrow, what exactly would I still own?

**The handle to remember:** every venue is a traffic deal — rent buyers until you've built
your own.
