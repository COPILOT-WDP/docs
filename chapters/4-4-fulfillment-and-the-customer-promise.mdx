One operator came back from a weekend to 78 orders waiting in his seller portal. He started
pulling cards after dinner; at 10:00 p.m. he had packed 52, and by about 11:00 all 78 were
staged for the post office. Another packs until 1 a.m. — when his customers get an "order is
being prepared" email at midnight, that's him, still at the table.

Neither man was making money in those hours — the margin was locked weeks earlier, on the buy
(Chapter 2.1). They were keeping the promise the sale made.

## The principle

**Shipping is part of the product.** The customer didn't buy a card; they bought a card
*arriving* — on time, in the listed condition, in packaging that did its job. The envelope is
the only piece of your business they will ever touch, so to them the envelope *is* the
business.

The economics follow. Operators rank the drivers of marketplace sales: price first, listing
count second, feedback third. Feedback is a sales input — which makes fulfillment a marketing
channel wearing a postage stamp. One operator has held 100% positive feedback across 15,000
orders. That's not a personality trait; it's an asset, built one envelope at a time.

## Pack to the price

The rookie error runs in both directions. Armor every order and you burn margin a dollar at a
time; armor nothing and one mangled box erases a month. The documented system is a ladder:
**pack to the price, not the card.**

| Order value | Documented packaging | Cost per order |
|---|---|---|
| **Under ~$20 — the default** | Windowed plain white envelope: cards in a team bag, taped to the packing slip, address showing through the window. Top loader only when the stack needs stiffness. One stamp under an ounce; ounce stamps above. | **~$0.85–0.86** |
| $20–50 — judgment zone | The platform recommends tracking from $20. One operator still ships a 17-card, $32 order in the envelope and "very rarely" has issues. Your risk tolerance, your call. | ~$0.86 + extra stamps |
| **~$50–150 — the upgrade** | Documented instinct kicks in at $60–70: team bag between top loaders, wrapped in the packing slip, into a bubble mailer with a tracked label. Tracking is required over $50 anyway. | **~$5.50–6** |
| $150+ | Same, plus signature confirmation. | +$4–6 |
| $300–500+ | Verify before you ship: a text at $300, a phone call and address confirmation at $500. | Minutes, not dollars |
| Sealed product | Boxes by size: 6x6x4 for a booster box, 8x8x4 for one ETB (~2 lb), 8x8x8 for two, 16x10x4 for premium collection boxes. Bubble wrap, packing paper, shake test before sealing. Double-wrap the expensive stuff. | ~$6–9 to ship |

About 80% of one high-volume operator's orders leave in the bottom tier, and the mechanics are
learnable in a night — five sleeved cards per team bag keeps an envelope thin enough for the
mail machine. His standard for the cheap tier: safe, secure, through the machine — then move on.

Why so stingy at the bottom? Volume math. Tracking runs about $6; put it on every order over
$20 and five orders eat $30. The sweat runs the other way at the top: shipping a 21-inch,
three-pound box cost $35 — $52 once — and turned a planned $20 wholesale profit into a $25–30
loss. Two supply rules ride along: round dimensions up (a documented $8 penalty for half an
inch), and buy in bulk — $3 retail boxes kill an order's margin; bulk small boxes run about
66 cents.

> **Operators disagree.** One school self-insures: no tracking below the line, refund the
> rare loss, keep stamping — across thousands of envelopes the documented loss rate is slim,
> and $6 tracking on 86-cent shipping costs more than the occasional refund. The other school
> armors: after thieves cut open six shipped cases, retaped them, and the insurance claim was
> denied — the packages were "delivered" —
> one operator switched carriers and now insures and overnights every master case. Another
> refuses to ship anything he can't insure: too valuable to insure means fly it or the buyer
> picks it up. Both are right at their ticket size. Resolve it with one question: **what does
> one lost package cost you, and can your bankroll absorb it (Chapter 5.2)?** Below that
> number, self-insure. Above it, armor up.

## Refund them, move on

Mail fails. At volume you will get lost cards, bent cards, and condition disputes (Chapter
4.3 is how you earn the benefit of the doubt). The playbook is boring and nearly identical
across operators:

1. **Respond fast and apologize.** Even when it isn't your fault.
2. **Ask for a few extra days.** Cheap envelopes quote ten to fifteen business days; 99% of the
   time, buyers say yes.
3. **Still missing or damaged? Refund. Move on.** Don't relitigate, don't let frustration reach
   the customer.
4. **Only after the customer is whole** may you block a problem buyer — one operator's rule:
   never block anybody before taking care of them.

Why so generous? First, you'd lose anyway — platforms side with the customer in condition
disputes. Second, a handled problem converts: refund the buyer and the platform removes
negative feedback — one operator says 100% of the time. Third, the stories compound: one
operator refunded a $700 order a carrier lost for a month rather than make the buyer keep
waiting; another lost a $600 card, collected about $100 of insurance, and ate the difference.
The doctrine underneath: losing $20 on one transaction is fine; losing your reputation
to save it is not. In a hobby where the customer-service bar is documented as low, taking care
of people is the cheapest differentiation you can buy.

## Speed is the promise

Transit time isn't yours; handling time — order to mailbox — is, and it's the whole lever.
The documented habits: ship next day and call it what it is, customer service. Set an order
cutoff (11 a.m. or 2 p.m.) and a fixed cadence — daily, every other day, whatever works for
you and your customers — so one-day handling doesn't cost you sleep. A card that landed five
days after the order got chalked up as a "W for fast shipping." When one operator was too sick
to pack, he pulled product off his store — never list what you can't send.

Speed shows up where repeat money shows up: the store with customers who reorder at
$2,000–$5,000 a time is the same store confirming addresses by phone and shipping next day.
And if volume ever swamps you, you already own the throttle: one operator manages how many
hours he spends shipping by where he prices his cards.

## The part you must not hate

Now the tough love. Fulfillment is the treadmill of this business. The documented pace: thirty
seconds for a standard order, under a minute for a big one — five to ten minutes per order is
called unacceptable at volume. A volume seller clears 40–50 orders in a two-to-three-hour
evening window, after the day job, after the kids are down. This is not the montage part of
the job. This *is* the job.

Chapter 1.4 set a validation gate at your first ~20 orders; this is what the gate validates.
One operator's advice on business entities: sell and ship first — find out whether you enjoy
stuffing envelopes and fielding messages about a nick on a corner before you spend a dollar
on structure. If your idea of a good evening is not stuffing envelopes with cards, you
just learned something valuable, cheap. Call it **the good-evening test.** It gets lighter —
30, 50, 100 orders in, operators say shipping stops feeling consequential, and systems
compress it (Chapter 6.1) — but it never disappears. Don't wait for a perfect method, either:
ship, and let the reviews tell you whether the packaging held.

## In practice

Before your next listing batch:

- [ ] What's your packaging tier for each price band — and its per-order cost, to the cent?
  (It feeds your cost stack, Chapter 2.2.)
- [ ] What's your handling promise, and the order cutoff hour that protects your sleep?
- [ ] Is your lost-package script written before you need it? (Apologize, ask for days, refund.)
- [ ] Can your bankroll absorb your most expensive listing vanishing uninsured? (Chapter 5.2.
  If not: armor it, or don't list it.)
- [ ] After 20 orders: has any envelope-stuffing evening felt like a good evening? Be honest —
  this is Chapter 1.4's question wearing a stamp.

**The handle to remember:** shipping is part of the product. Pack to the price, ship it the
next day, and when the mail fails — refund them, move on.
