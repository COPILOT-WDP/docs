At every big card show, the same offer works the aisles: 85% of market for the whole booth.
One operator did the math out loud. He has a card marked \$100. You offer him \$90 — cash,
right now. "If I paid 85% for that, I'm probably not gonna take it." He needs about \$95,
"maybe budget like 93," because the table, the gas, and the hotel are already eating the
spread, and he's "gotta try to make at least 10%."

Read that again. A real buyer offered him 90% of market, and his own math forced him to say
no. The \$90 wasn't a bad offer. The \$85 was a bad buy.

## The principle

**Price to move, not to max.** A card's theoretical maximum — the top recent sale, the highest
listing — is not a price. It's a lottery ticket priced in shelf time. Selling is a math
problem with four inputs: sold comps, the discount that makes a buyer act now, your venue's
fees, and what you paid. You look up the first three. The fourth you already chose —
[Chapter 2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy).

The whole model fits in one documented sentence: buy collections at 70% of market, sell at 5%
under market, pay 5% in fees. Call it **the 70/95/5 engine.** On a \$100 card:

| The engine, on a \$100 card | |
|---|---|
| Market value (recent *sold* prices) | \$100.00 |
| Your list price — 5% under market | \$95.00 |
| Platform fees (~5%, lean venue) | −\$4.75 |
| You collect | \$90.25 |
| You paid — the 70% anchor | −\$70.00 |
| **You keep** | **\$20.25** |

About 20 points of market value — roughly 29% on the cash you deployed. Two footnotes: at
~15% marketplace fees the same sale keeps about \$10.75 (your cost stack decides your pricing
room — [Chapter 2.2](/chapters/2-2-the-real-cost-stack)), and shipping and supplies still come out of that \$20.

Why give away the 5%? Because to a buyer scanning a hundred listings, "if it's under market,
it's a deal." Priced about 5% under, one operator's booster boxes "fly off the shelves" —
while a box he listed at roughly 90% of market just sat. The last 5% of a card's value is the
most expensive 5% to collect: you pay for it in shelf time, and shelf time is frozen cash
([Chapter 2.3](/chapters/2-3-cashflow-is-oxygen)). One honest limit: the discount multiplies demand, it doesn't create it — one
documented listing sat at \$95 against a \$99–\$100 market, "nobody's buying them."

Back to the show floor. Operators who buy at 80–85% report the same consequence: they're
forced to charge full market to cover taxes, fees, and chargeback risk — to wait for the one
buyer who pays sticker. Buy at 70 and the math flips: "now I can sell these for like 93 to
95% still make my percentage." Overpaying doesn't just shrink margin. It confiscates your
pricing freedom — the freedom to say yes to the \$90 offer.

## You don't need to be the lowest listing

The discount buys speed. It is not an identity. Two documented failure modes flank the 5%:

**Pricing too low.** One operator's first-month tuition: he listed at — sometimes under — the
lowest listing on every card. His verdict: "the amount of money that I missed out on in that
first month." The lesson: "you don't need to be quite that aggressive... they'll move fast if
you price them like around market." Fair prices move cards; the bottom listing just moves
them for less.

**Discounting as a strategy.** A documented seller type prices 5–15% under market to become
the affordable seller and build a reputation. The documented verdict: it doesn't scale.
Repeat buyers are "not necessarily looking for the cheapest price. They just want
consistency" — and the reputation evaporates the day you return to market prices. Five
percent under is a tool. Fifteen percent under is a donation with a storefront.

One place 5% under costs you nothing: your own store. Operators price 5% under market there
*because their fees are lower there* — the discount comes out of fees they didn't pay, not
out of margin:

| Same \$100 card | List price | Fees | You keep |
|---|---|---|---|
| Marketplace, full market | \$100 | ~15% | \$85.00 |
| Your own store, 5% under | \$95 | ~5% | \$90.25 |

The buyer saves \$5. You keep \$5.25 more. One documented month of this — the same statement
that opened [Chapter 2.2](/chapters/2-2-the-real-cost-stack): \$17,914 in own-store sales, \$987 in fees — 5.5%. The discount also trains customers to buy from you instead of the
marketplace — the start of owning a channel ([Chapter 4.2](/chapters/4-2-choosing-where-to-sell)).

## The fast sale or the wait

Take the quick offer, or hold for full price? The card can't answer that. Your cash position
can.

| Your cash position | The move | The documented version |
|---|---|---|
| A deal is available, capital tied up | Liquidate near cost — even \$5–\$10 underwater a box | "Cash flow is king. You ain't got no business if you ain't got no cash." |
| Need inventory cash this week | Undercut by a hair, not a mile | Drop the price \$5, sold in twenty minutes to an hour, cash straight into the next collection |
| Fair offer in hand, bought right | Take it and move | Bought at \$100, worth \$120, sold at \$110 on the spot: "I just made 10%." |
| No deal pending, desirable inventory | Hold your price | Three to six months on a desirable card is normal; one operator has waited nine |
| No deal pending, no cash need | Don't panic at week six | "If you don't have things coming in, why are you fire selling your inventory?" |

> **Operators disagree.** One school is pure velocity: take the 7–8%, rotate into better
> product — "I'm all about the volume. I'm a move, move, move." The other school treats a
> too-fast sale as an error: if a card sells within a couple hours of listing, "I probably
> priced it too low" — and holds desirable inventory for months without flinching.
> Both schools are pricing the same thing: the value of their next dollar. Resolve it with one
> question: **what will the cash from this sale do tomorrow?** If it funds a buy you'd
> otherwise miss, the fast sale is mathematically correct — take it near cost if you have to.
> If it would sit in your checking account, the wait costs nothing. Speed is bought with
> margin. Only buy it when you need it.

One diagnostic: if you keep needing to liquidate inventory within a month of buying it,
you're not unlucky — "you might be cash poor." That's a bankroll problem ([Chapter 5.2](/chapters/5-2-bankroll-discipline)), not a
pricing problem.

## Sales are not earnings

Every documented operator eventually says the same sentence: **"sales does not equal
profit."** One announces a \$120,000 month and immediately adds, "That's in sales, by the way,
not profit." Believe them — then apply it to yourself.

| Documented sales number | What the operator actually kept |
|---|---|
| \$225,000 in a year | ~\$40,000 — profit ran 15–25% of sales |
| \$5,600 in a weekend | roughly \$800–\$1,000 |
| \$55,200 in a first quarter | possibly a paper loss for the year, pending the accountant |

Build the reflex: any sales figure — a thumbnail, a competitor's flex, your own dashboard —
multiply by 0.15–0.25 to estimate the money. The failure mode is documented too: "they are
looking at revenue as profit." Spend \$100 on an ETB, sell it for \$110, feel like a winner —
"maybe you actually lost money if you had to pay fees and shipping." [Chapter 2.3](/chapters/2-3-cashflow-is-oxygen) warned that
cash flow isn't profit; this is the mirror error. Sales is the scoreboard the platform shows
you. Margin is the game.

## In practice

Before you list — or relist — answer all six:

- [ ] Is this price built from **sold** comps at ~95% of market, or from the listing I hope comes true?
- [ ] Could I say yes to a 90%-of-market offer today and still profit? If not, the problem was the buy ([Chapter 2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy)).
- [ ] Am I the lowest listing? What exactly am I paying for that title?
- [ ] If this sold in the next hour, where does the cash go — into a deal, or into a bank balance?
- [ ] Is anything I bought last month already on the chopping block? ([Chapter 5.2](/chapters/5-2-bankroll-discipline).)
- [ ] Am I quoting sales as earnings — to others, or to myself? Multiply by 0.15–0.25 first.

**The handle to remember:** price to move, not to max. The maximum is a number in your head.
The moving price is money in the drawer.
