An operator with distributor invoices coming due sold Evolving Skies by the case at $130 a
box and let Fusion Strike go below its $100 market. On purpose. Years later, boxes from that
set sat listed at $2,600 apiece. He doesn't tell the story as a mistake. The cheap sales
paid the invoices, the invoices kept the pipeline open, and the pipeline grew into an
operation that has since sent a single supplier millions of dollars. His summary is one
sentence: you need cash flow to build a business.

## The principle

**Cashflow is oxygen.** A card business is a loop: cash becomes inventory, inventory becomes
cash, slightly more cash buys slightly more inventory. Anything that interrupts the loop —
money frozen in slow product, money stuck in pending payouts, money pulled out for yourself —
is a hand over the business's mouth.

No principle in this manual has stronger consensus. Operators running completely different
models say it in almost the same words: "cash flow is the name of the game." "Cash flow is
king — you ain't got no business if you ain't got no cash." "You need cash flow to build a
business." They even share a handle for the operating style it demands, credited to each
other on camera: **churn and burn.** Buy, sell fast, buy again.

## Three numbers, one register

Sales, profit, and cash are three different numbers, and beginners quote the wrong one.

$20,000 sold is not $20,000 made. An operator with close to half a million dollars in sales
puts it flatly: buy an elite trainer box for $100, sell it for $110, and you made $10 —
before fees and shipping, maybe less after. His real margins run 15–25% of sales. Another
operator announced a $120,000 month and corrected himself in the same breath: that's sales,
not profit.

And profit still isn't cash. Marketplace payouts lag: one operator watched $8,000 in
completed sales take two weeks to reach his account.

| The number | What it actually is | Documented reality check |
|---|---|---|
| **Sales** | Dollars through the register | A $120,000 month, announced with the correction "not profit" built in |
| **Profit** | Sales minus inventory cost minus the cost stack (Chapter 2.2) | ~$13,000 kept on a $100,000+ month; operators document 15–25% of sales |
| **Cash** | What's in the account *today* | $8,000 in sold cards, two weeks from spendable |

Chapter 2.4 covers the pricing math that turns sales into earnings. This chapter is about
the third column — the only number that buys the next collection.

## Velocity beats margin

The move that separates operators from holders: **take the small gain and keep the money
moving.** One operator says it directly — he would rather make 7% or 8% and rotate into
better product than let inventory sit as a display piece or an "investment." Another
accepts 8–10% specifically because it speeds up cash flow, asking the only question that
matters about a fat sticker price: how long will you hold to get it?

The arithmetic is the whole argument. A documented example: flip $1,000 for a 10% gain,
reinvest the $1,100, and you can be at roughly $1,250–$1,300 in under thirty days — instead
of waiting months or years for the "easy" $1,500. Extend that logic across a year:

| The play | Margin | A year later, $1,000 is |
|---|---|---|
| **Churn and burn, monthly turns** | 7–8% per turn | **~$2,250–$2,500** |
| Churn and burn at documented target | ~15% per turn | ~$5,300 |
| Hold for the big exit | +30%, once | $1,300 |
| Hold for the "easy" double | +50%, someday | $1,500 — eventually, maybe |

*Illustrative compounding at full redeployment, net of your cost stack (Chapter 2.2). The
margins are documented: ~15% targeted, 10% accepted on weak product, 20% "ideal but
uncommon." Monthly turns are realistic for liquid product, fantasy for slow product.*

Every turn also compounds *you*: more comps, more sellers, more capital for the next band
up. One operator credits churn and burn with taking him from limited capital to $50,000
collection buys; another describes the ladder plainly — sell $10,000 of product, turn it
into $12,000, buy bigger collections.

Velocity is even why operators sell at a loss on purpose — one documents selling below cost
because "cash flow is vital for a business." Before taking the hit, ask the two documented
questions: am I actually cash-poor? Is this inventory costing me better buys? If either is
yes, losing $5–$10 a box to breathe is a trade, not a failure. But if you *routinely* must
liquidate within a month of buying, the problem is bankroll, not inventory — Chapter 5.2.

## The year-one loop

The fastest documented growth curves share one boring feature: for the first year, the
owner took nothing out. Operators reach this independently: one calls taking nothing out in
year one simply "reasonable," because a business you're draining isn't growing. Another
didn't pay himself for over a year. A third keeps every dollar of sold inventory in the
business.

What the loop produces: $22,000 of starting inventory compounding to roughly six figures of
product and cash within a year. A documented buy-sell-reinvest cycle claimed to grow a
bankroll to $10,000 and eventually $100,000. Reselling is an opportunity game; every dollar
of capital is a ticket to the next deal.

Every owner draw therefore has a price tag denominated in deals. One operator pulled money
out to finish his basement — a fine, adult thing to do — and could no longer buy the
$20,000-plus collections he'd been buying. Growth slowed, documented and admitted. The
lesson isn't "never pay yourself"; it's that a draw is a trade — name the deal it might
cost you first.

## The closet is not a business

Now the anti-principle — the most expensive mistake in the evidence base: treating
inventory as an investment portfolio in your early years.

The documented test is one question: **are you running a business or are you running an
investment?** Product appreciating in a closet is investing — even if you call yourself a
store. And appreciation is not profit: one operator with a $45,000 sealed position says out
loud that he hasn't made a dollar until he liquidates it. A paper gain ships no orders and
pays no invoices.

One operator's rule draws the line in years: **no inventory set aside as investment in your
first five years** — unless the business already has genuine cash surplus. Five years is his
number; the logic is everyone's. Early on, your capital has a job.

The tuition stories are brutal. One seller put $30,000 — nearly all his working capital —
into a single hot set, then had to dump it at a loss to cover a distributor invoice; his
documented lesson was to keep enough capital liquid to survive his own commitments. Another
sat on roughly 150 cases of a slow product while an $80,000 invoice came due; he paused all
collection buying and passed a $200,000 collection that would have put him in the red —
opportunity cost he puts in the tens of thousands a month. Unsold inventory never feels
like a loss. There's no red number on a shelf. That's what makes it dangerous.

> **Operators disagree.** One school churns everything: a store is a pipe, not a vault.
> The other blends: one operator, grown to six figures, now holds back 10% of product —
> 20% "would be nice" — and another bought his dream home with investment inventory that
> sat four years. Both outcomes are documented. But watch the sequencing: the dream-home
> operator churned for years first and *still* sells at a loss when cash flow demands it;
> the blender started holding only once the store could fund it from surplus. Nobody held
> first and built second. Resolve it with one question: **if this money were locked in a
> box for three years, would my business ever miss it?** If it would, that isn't an
> investment — it's working capital wearing a costume.

## In practice

Before the month closes, you should be able to check every box:

- [ ] Can I state this month's three numbers — sales, profit, cash — without guessing? (If
      I only know sales, I know the least useful one.)
- [ ] What share of my bankroll is in product that hasn't moved in 60 days?
- [ ] If a good $2,000 collection appeared tomorrow, could I buy it without a fire sale?
- [ ] Year one: is every dollar of profit going back into inventory? If not, what deal is
      the draw costing me?
- [ ] Is anything on my shelf an "investment" I haven't admitted to? Business or closet?
- [ ] Do I know when marketplace money actually lands — or am I counting pending payouts
      as cash?

**The handle to remember:** cashflow is oxygen. Margin is how well you breathe. Cash flow is
whether you're breathing at all.
