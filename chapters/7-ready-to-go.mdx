Twenty chapters compress to one test: **can you answer these questions with numbers and
dates, not intentions?** Each one names the chapter that teaches it. If an answer is missing,
that's not failure — that's your reading list.

## The readiness checklist

**The mind (Part 1)**
- [ ] Collection and inventory live in different places, and flip-or-keep is decided at the door. (1.1)
- [ ] My month-one success number is roughly zero, plus an education. (1.2)
- [ ] My seed number is written down — sold comps, minus fees, no debt — and something is listed *today*. (1.3)
- [ ] I've shipped 20 orders before spending a dollar on structure or tools. (1.4)

**The math (Part 2)**
- [ ] I offer from sold comps, in the right band, around the 70% anchor — for a reason, not a feeling. (2.1)
- [ ] I know my all-in cost stack to the point: lean ~8%, typical ~15% — mine, measured. (2.2)
- [ ] I can state this month's three numbers — sales, profit, cash — without guessing. (2.3)
- [ ] I price to move at ~95% of market, and I never quote sales as earnings. (2.4)

**The product (Part 3)**
- [ ] I know which class holds my money — singles, sealed, bulk, or slabs — because I chose it. (3.1)
- [ ] My class matches what I actually have more of: hours or dollars. One lane, run deep. (3.2)

**The craft (Part 4)**
- [ ] I'm on the sourcing rung my bankroll covers, and sellers know I'm buying. (4.1)
- [ ] I know whether I'm renting traffic or bringing it — and what I'd still own if the venue vanished. (4.2)
- [ ] My floor pile is worth $0, because only listed inventory sells. (4.3)
- [ ] Packaging is tiered to price, the lost-package script is written, and packing evenings are still good evenings. (4.4)

**The protection (Part 5)**
- [ ] Business money has its own account, and money out comes back with margin. (5.1)
- [ ] Every buy is existing business cash — bankroll is money that's allowed to lose. (5.2)
- [ ] I can reach my customers without my main platform, and my inventory turns before the market can turn on it. (5.3)

**The growth (Part 6)**
- [ ] My process is written down — I have a system, not habits — and I buy the rung I'm standing on. (6.1)
- [ ] I've decided what content is to me: the moat I'm building, or the fourth job I'm skipping — on purpose. (6.2)
- [ ] Full-time is a threshold on a ledger, not an escape plan. (6.3)

## The first ninety days

The documented trajectories in Chapter 1.2 all share one shape. Here it is as a plan:

**Days 1–30 — Validate.** Sell what you already own. List something the first night; pick the
venue by coin flip if you must. Ship your first 20 orders, handle one condition complaint,
and answer the only question that matters yet: *do I tolerate this work?* Spend nothing on
structure. A documented first month: $1,481 in sales, 83 orders, two-thirds of a month —
and possibly a small loss. That was a pass, not a fail.

**Days 31–60 — Pay tuition.** Compute your cost stack from your own first orders (2.2). Make
your first real buys in the right band (2.1) at your bankroll's rung (3.2, 5.2). Expect to
break even; you're buying lessons with receipts. Start the three-numbers habit: sales,
profit, cash, every week.

**Days 61–90 — Close the loop.** Every dollar of profit goes back into inventory (2.3). Floor
pile to zero, always be listing (4.3). Pick your lane and say it in one sentence (3.2). Write
down your buy-sort-list-pack-ship process (6.1). If the quarter ends with a system, a lane,
and a turning bankroll — you have a business. Small is fine. **Big is just boring, repeated.**

## The last word

You will overpay for a collection. A box will sit. A platform will change its rules in a
month you didn't choose. None of that is the test. The test is whether your next buy is
sharper, your cash keeps moving, and your evenings still feel like yours.

**Always be learning. Always be listing.**
