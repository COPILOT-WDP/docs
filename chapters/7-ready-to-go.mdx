Twenty chapters compress to one test: **can you answer these questions with numbers and
dates, not intentions?** Each one names the chapter that teaches it. If an answer is missing,
that's not failure — that's your reading list.

## The readiness checklist

**The mind (Part 1)**
- [ ] Collection and inventory live in different places, and flip-or-keep is decided at the door. ([1.1](/chapters/1-1-the-merchant-mindset))
- [ ] My month-one success number is roughly zero, plus an education. ([1.2](/chapters/1-2-what-success-actually-looks-like))
- [ ] My seed number is written down — sold comps, minus fees, no debt — and something is listed *today*. ([1.3](/chapters/1-3-the-three-ingredients))
- [ ] I've shipped 20 orders before spending a dollar on structure or tools. ([1.4](/chapters/1-4-validate-before-you-build))

**The math (Part 2)**
- [ ] I offer from sold comps, in the right band, around the 70% anchor — for a reason, not a feeling. ([2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy))
- [ ] I know my all-in cost stack to the point: lean ~8%, typical ~15% — mine, measured. ([2.2](/chapters/2-2-the-real-cost-stack))
- [ ] I can state this month's three numbers — sales, profit, cash — without guessing. ([2.3](/chapters/2-3-cashflow-is-oxygen))
- [ ] I price to move at ~95% of market, and I never quote sales as earnings. ([2.4](/chapters/2-4-selling-is-a-math-problem))

**The product (Part 3)**
- [ ] I know which class holds my money — singles, sealed, bulk, or slabs — because I chose it. ([3.1](/chapters/3-1-the-four-product-classes))
- [ ] My class matches what I actually have more of: hours or dollars. One lane, run deep. ([3.2](/chapters/3-2-matching-class-to-capital))

**The craft (Part 4)**
- [ ] I'm on the sourcing rung my bankroll covers, and sellers know I'm buying. ([4.1](/chapters/4-1-sourcing-the-ladder))
- [ ] I know whether I'm renting traffic or bringing it — and what I'd still own if the venue vanished. ([4.2](/chapters/4-2-choosing-where-to-sell))
- [ ] My floor pile is worth \$0, because only listed inventory sells. ([4.3](/chapters/4-3-listing-and-inventory-discipline))
- [ ] Packaging is tiered to price, the lost-package script is written, and packing evenings are still good evenings. ([4.4](/chapters/4-4-fulfillment-and-the-customer-promise))

**The protection (Part 5)**
- [ ] Business money has its own account, and money out comes back with margin. ([5.1](/chapters/5-1-business-money-hygiene))
- [ ] Every buy is existing business cash — bankroll is money that's allowed to lose. ([5.2](/chapters/5-2-bankroll-discipline))
- [ ] I can reach my customers without my main platform, and my inventory turns before the market can turn on it. ([5.3](/chapters/5-3-the-risk-map))

**The growth (Part 6)**
- [ ] My process is written down — I have a system, not habits — and I buy the rung I'm standing on. ([6.1](/chapters/6-1-systems-before-scale))
- [ ] I've decided what content is to me: the moat I'm building, or the fourth job I'm skipping — on purpose. ([6.2](/chapters/6-2-content-is-a-moat))
- [ ] Full-time is a threshold on a ledger, not an escape plan. ([6.3](/chapters/6-3-going-full-time-or-not))

## The first ninety days

The documented trajectories in [Chapter 1.2](/chapters/1-2-what-success-actually-looks-like) all share one shape. Here it is as a plan:

**Days 1–30 — Validate.** Sell what you already own. List something the first night; pick the
venue by coin flip if you must. Ship your first 20 orders, handle one condition complaint,
and answer the only question that matters yet: *do I tolerate this work?* Spend nothing on
structure. A documented first month: \$1,481 in sales, 83 orders, two-thirds of a month —
and possibly a small loss. That was a pass, not a fail.

**Days 31–60 — Pay tuition.** Compute your cost stack from your own first orders ([2.2](/chapters/2-2-the-real-cost-stack)). Make
your first real buys in the right band ([2.1](/chapters/2-1-you-make-money-when-you-buy)) at your bankroll's rung ([3.2](/chapters/3-2-matching-class-to-capital), [5.2](/chapters/5-2-bankroll-discipline)). Expect to
break even; you're buying lessons with receipts. Start the three-numbers habit: sales,
profit, cash, every week.

**Days 61–90 — Close the loop.** Every dollar of profit goes back into inventory ([2.3](/chapters/2-3-cashflow-is-oxygen)). Floor
pile to zero, always be listing ([4.3](/chapters/4-3-listing-and-inventory-discipline)). Pick your lane and say it in one sentence ([3.2](/chapters/3-2-matching-class-to-capital)). Write
down your buy-sort-list-pack-ship process ([6.1](/chapters/6-1-systems-before-scale)). If the quarter ends with a system, a lane,
and a turning bankroll — you have a business. Small is fine. **Big is just boring, repeated.**

## The last word

You will overpay for a collection. A box will sit. A platform will change its rules in a
month you didn't choose. None of that is the test. The test is whether your next buy is
sharper, your cash keeps moving, and your evenings still feel like yours.

**Always be learning. Always be listing.**
