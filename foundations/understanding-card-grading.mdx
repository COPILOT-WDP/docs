A professionally graded Pokémon card in a sealed slab isn't just a protected card — it's a certified asset. Graded cards routinely sell for multiples of their raw counterparts because buyers trust the independent condition assessment, making every transaction faster and more confident. If you plan to sell high-value singles or build inventory in the premium collector segment, understanding the grading ecosystem is non-negotiable.

## What Card Grading Is

Professional card grading is the process of submitting a raw card to an independent third-party company that evaluates its physical condition, assigns a numeric grade, seals it in a tamper-evident plastic case (called a "slab"), and returns it to you with a certification label. That label includes the card name, set, grade, and a unique certification number buyers can verify online.

The grade transforms a subjective "this looks near mint" into an objective, market-trusted standard — which is exactly why graded cards command premiums and trade with higher buyer confidence.

## The Three Major Grading Companies

<Accordion title="PSA — Professional Sports Authenticator">
  PSA is the most recognized name in trading card grading worldwide. A PSA 10 "Gem Mint" is the gold standard for collectible card value, and PSA-graded Pokémon cards consistently achieve the highest sale prices at auction.

  **Pros**
  - Highest brand recognition and resale value premium
  - Largest population registry (easy to research comp sales)
  - PSA 10 on a key card can multiply raw value by 10–20x

  **Cons**
  - Notoriously strict grading standards — many cards submitted at 10 come back as 9s
  - Turnaround times have ranged from weeks to over a year depending on service level
  - Pricing tiers can be expensive for bulk submissions

  **Best for:** High-value vintage cards, key chase rares, investment-grade graded cards
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="BGS — Beckett Grading Services">
  Beckett offers a unique sub-grade system, breaking condition into four components — Centering, Corners, Edges, and Surface — each scored individually, with the overall grade derived from those sub-grades. A BGS 9.5 "Gem Mint" with four 10 sub-grades earns a coveted "Black Label Pristine" designation.

  **Pros**
  - Sub-grades add transparency and appeal to detail-focused collectors
  - BGS 10 "Pristine" and Black Label cards are extremely rare and highly valued
  - Beckett has strong recognition in the sports card world, which crosses over to Pokémon

  **Cons**
  - Sub-grade system can be frustrating — a single 9 sub-grade prevents a 9.5 overall
  - Generally considered slightly less liquid than PSA in the Pokémon market specifically
  - Blue label slabs can look less premium than PSA's clean aesthetic to some buyers

  **Best for:** Modern cards where you want sub-grade transparency, collectors who prize BGS 10 designations
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="CGC — Certified Guaranty Company">
  CGC entered the trading card market in 2020 and quickly built a strong reputation for consistent grading and faster turnaround times than PSA or BGS. CGC cards have grown rapidly in market acceptance and are now widely traded on eBay and major platforms.

  **Pros**
  - Faster and often more affordable turnaround than PSA
  - Consistent, transparent grading standards
  - Strong growth in market acceptance — CGC 10s are increasingly liquid
  - Good option for mid-tier cards where PSA fees wouldn't be justified

  **Cons**
  - PSA still commands the highest per-card premium for most key Pokémon cards
  - Smaller population registry than PSA makes comp research harder
  - Relatively newer entrant — long-term brand equity still being established

  **Best for:** Mid-value cards, bulk grading submissions, sellers who want faster turnaround
</Accordion>

## The Grading Scale: 1–10

| Grade | Label | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 10 | Gem Mint | Perfect or near-perfect card with no visible flaws under close inspection |
| 9 | Mint | Minor imperfection(s) only visible under scrutiny — excellent centering and crisp corners |
| 8 | Near Mint–Mint | Very slight wear — a minor corner touch or very slight surface imperfection allowed |
| 7 | Near Mint | Light play wear visible; corners show minor rounding but no creases or major damage |
| 6 | Excellent–Mint | Moderate wear including slight corner fraying, minor scratches, or minor print defects |
| 5 | Excellent | Noticeable wear on corners and edges; surface may show scuffs but no major damage |
| 4 | Very Good–Excellent | Obvious wear throughout; corners moderately frayed, surface wear clearly visible |
| 3 | Very Good | Heavy wear, possible creasing; card remains structurally intact |
| 2 | Good | Significant damage — heavy creases, major surface scratching, or edge tears |
| 1 | Poor / Authentic | Heavily damaged but authenticated as genuine; has collector value for authentication only |

## Assessing Raw Card Condition Before Submitting

Before paying grading fees, pre-screen every card carefully under good lighting. Graders evaluate four key areas:

**Centering** — Measure the border on all four sides. PSA's guideline for a 10 is approximately 55/45 or better on front and 75/25 or better on back. Use a centering tool or a ruler to check. Off-center cards rarely grade above an 8.

**Corners** — Use a loupe or magnifying glass. Look for fraying, nicking, or rounding at all four corners. Even a single dinged corner typically drops a card below a 9.

**Edges** — Run your finger along all four edges and examine under light. Chips, indentations, or roughness along the edge line signal an 8 or below.

**Surface** — Tilt the card under a direct light source to reveal scratches, print lines, haze, or indentations that aren't obvious straight-on. Surface issues are the most common reason cards fail to reach a 10.

## Grading Costs and Turnaround Times

Grading fees vary by company and service tier. As a general reference:

| Company | Economy Tier | Standard Tier | Express Tier |
|---|---|---|---|
| PSA | ~$20–25/card | ~$50/card | $150+/card |
| BGS | ~$22/card | ~$50/card | $150+/card |
| CGC | ~$18/card | ~$35/card | $75+/card |

Turnaround times fluctuate with submission volume. Economy tiers can run 3–9 months; express tiers can return cards in 1–4 weeks. Always check the current estimated turnaround on each company's website before submitting, as these change frequently.

<Warning>
  Grading fees eat directly into your profit margin. On a card worth $30 raw, a $25 grading fee means you need the slab to sell for nearly double the raw price just to break even — and that's before factoring in shipping to and from the grader, insurance, platform fees, and the risk of receiving a lower-than-expected grade. Only submit cards where the expected grade-up premium comfortably exceeds all-in costs.
</Warning>

## When to Grade vs. Sell Raw

Grading makes financial sense when:

- The card is worth $75 or more in near-mint raw condition
- You have strong reason to believe it will grade a 9 or 10 (based on careful pre-screening)
- The card is a key collectible (Charizard, Pikachu, popular full arts) where graded premiums are well-established
- You're building long-term inventory and can absorb the capital lockup during turnaround time

Selling raw is often smarter when:

- The card has visible wear that will likely result in a grade of 7 or below
- The raw card value is under $50 and fees represent more than 30–40% of that value
- You need liquidity quickly and can't wait months for graded returns

<Tip>
  Pre-screen cards by photographing them under a bright desk lamp at multiple angles before committing to a submission. Compare your photos to the population reports on each grading company's registry — if similar cards in similar condition consistently grade 8 or below, save your fees and sell the card raw.
</Tip>
